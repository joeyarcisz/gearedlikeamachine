Renting gear in DFW is easy. Renting the wrong gear and discovering it on set is expensive. The difference between a smooth shoot and a half-day scramble almost always traces back to a decision made three days before cameras rolled.

This is a working guide. Not a gear review. Not a vendor directory. A system for thinking through rental decisions the way experienced producers and DPs do.

## The First Decision: What Goes on the Truck

Every rental decision starts with the same question: does this piece of gear change the image, or does it support the image?

**Image-changing gear** is the camera body, lenses, and key lights. These are non-negotiable decisions that shape everything the client sees. Pick them based on the look, not the rate sheet.

**Image-supporting gear** is grip, expendables, monitors, follow focus, power distribution. This is where you optimize. Bring exactly what the script calls for and nothing else.

Most budget overruns on rentals happen when productions treat image-supporting gear like image-changing gear. They over-spec a grip truck because it feels safer, not because the job needs it.

Know the difference and budget accordingly.

## Camera Bodies: Stop Defaulting to What You Know

The DFW market is deep on camera inventory. ARRI Alexas, RED MONSTRO and RAPTOR, Sony VENICE and FX9, Blackmagic Cinema Camera 6K, Canon C70. Every major format is available for same-day or overnight rental.

The mistake most DPs make is defaulting to the camera they shoot on personally. Familiarity is real, but it is not always the right call for the job.

**Corporate talking head? One-day interview run?** The Sony FX9 with a solid Prime lens set is a stronger rental than an ALEXA Mini LF. The ARRI costs more per day, requires more technical handling, and the 4K ProRes output difference will not survive the client's YouTube compression anyway.

**Automotive or product commercial with high motion?** Now you want the ARRI or RED. The latitude matters. The highlight rolloff matters. The client is paying for a look that holds up in a graded deliverable on a broadcast timeline.

**Narrative short or branded content with cinematic intent?** ARRI Alexa or the Sony VENICE. The color science earns its rental rate.

Match the camera to the end deliverable, not to your preference.

## Lenses: The Part Most Productions Under-Budget

Lenses are the most undervalued line item in a rental budget, and also the one that most affects the image.

A $600/day camera body can produce extraordinary images with a $400/day prime lens set. The reverse is not true. Shooting a $600/day lens set on a $600/day camera produces average images.

The DFW rental market has strong inventory on:

- **Cooke S4/i Primes** (warm, organic, classic Cooke look)
- **ZEISS Supreme Primes** (clinical, sharp, modern commercial aesthetic)
- **Leica Summilux-C** (cinematic, gentle falloff, portrait-friendly)
- **Sigma Cine FF High Speed Primes** (capable budget option for productions under $15K total)
- **Anamorphics** (Hawk V-Lite, ARRI/ZEISS Master Anamorphics for 2.39:1 scope work)

Budget at minimum $200 to $450 per day for a useful prime set. Factor in lens support: matte box, follow focus, support rods. Those are separate line items and they add up.

Never show up to a rental house expecting to grab lenses at the last minute during busy season. Prime sets from reputable houses book out Thursday through Monday in DFW. Reserve early.

## Lighting: Think in Scenarios, Not SKUs

Production lighting rentals get complicated fast because the combinations are nearly infinite. Break it into three scenarios and it becomes manageable.

**Scenario A: Controlled Interior (Office, Studio, Warehouse)**

Single large soft source for key (12x12 frame with HMI or large LED panel), a fill card or 4x4 frame, a Kino Flo or tube light for background separation. Total: three or four units. Under $800/day for most corporate work.

**Scenario B: Run-and-Gun Multi-Location**

LED panels that can run on battery. ARRI SkyPanel S60 or LiteGear LITEMAT Plus series. No power drops, no extension cord runs, fast repositioning. The speed payoff matters more than the marginal light quality difference.

**Scenario C: Cinematic Exterior or Large Practical Set**

This is where HMI comes in. A 1.2K or 2.5K HMI through diffusion for daylight balancing. A 4K Fresnel for hard sunlight matching. Generator rental becomes necessary. Budget $300 to $600/day for the HMI alone, plus genny at $200 to $400/day depending on size.

Know your scenario before you open the rental catalog. Browsing a full grip and electric price list without a lighting plan is how productions accidentally rent $3,000 worth of gear they use for four hours.

## Grip: The Category Most People Get Wrong

Grip is the most over-rented and under-understood category in production.

A standard Dallas commercial day does not need a full 1-ton grip truck. It needs a C-stand package (12 to 15 stands), sandbags, a few flags and diffusion frames, a 4x8 ultra bounce, and a 12x12 frame if you're outdoors.

Over-rentals in grip come from fear. The DP does not want to be caught without a tool. The grip truck package feels comprehensive. But every item on the truck costs money, and half of it never leaves the truck on most corporate jobs.

**Start with the specific shots and work backward.** If the script calls for outdoor interviews and a product walk-and-talk, list the exact control you need for those specific shots. Rent that list. Add 20% for contingency. Stop there.

The exception is narrative work or large commercial productions where the unknown is genuinely unknown. On those, a stocked 1-ton is the right call because the shoot day has too many variables. But corporate and brand content rarely justifies the spend.

## The DFW Rental Market: What's Different Here

Dallas has a functional mid-tier rental market, not a deep one. This creates specific planning realities.

The major national rental houses, Lensrentals, Adorama Rentals, LensProToGo, ship to Texas with one-day transit from their East Coast or national fulfillment centers. For planned productions, this is often a better option than local-only sourcing. The inventory depth is unmatched and the QC processes at national houses are rigorous.

For same-day emergencies or camera-specific needs, local DFW rental houses fill the gap. Know both channels. Use them for what they're best at.

**Ship national for:**
- Premium prime lens sets (Cookes, Leicas, ZEISS Supremes)
- Specialty camera bodies (ARRI, VENICE)
- Anything booked more than five days out

**Source local for:**
- Same-day or next-day grip additions
- Expendables
- Generator rentals (shipping a genny makes no sense)
- Overflow items when the main package is already built

## The Insurance Conversation You Cannot Skip

Every production renting gear needs a Certificate of Insurance before the rental house hands over anything meaningful. This is not optional.

Production insurance covers rented equipment while in your care, custody, and control. A standard DICE (Domestic Inland Camera Equipment) policy or a single-production policy covers most rental situations. Rates run $150 to $400 for a multi-day production depending on total insured value.

The rental house's own damage waiver is not production insurance. It covers you against accidental damage at a premium rate, but it does not protect you against theft, loss, or third-party liability. Buy proper coverage.

If you operate regularly enough to rent gear monthly, an annual production insurance policy is cheaper than per-shoot coverage and removes the administrative friction from every project.

## Building the Rental Budget Line Item

Here is the practical math for a standard Dallas corporate video production:

**One-day corporate interview + b-roll:**
Camera body + lens set: $350 to $550/day. LED lighting package: $250 to $400/day. Grip package: $150 to $250/day. Monitor + playback: $100/day. Total camera and G&E rental: $850 to $1,300/day.

**Two-day commercial campaign:**
Camera body + prime set: $600 to $900/day. Lighting (HMI + LED): $600 to $900/day. Full grip package: $300 to $500/day. Support package (DIT, monitors, power): $200 to $350/day. Total: $1,700 to $2,650/day, or $3,400 to $5,300 for the two-day package.

These are real DFW market rates. Not aspirational. Not discounted. What you should expect to pay when you call a reputable house with a solid production.

## The Production Company Advantage

Here is the variable most budget sheets miss. When you hire a production company that owns its camera package, you do not pay rental markup on the primary camera system.

A DP or production company with a full Sony FX9 or ARRI Alexa system removes the camera body, lens set, and support package from your rental budget entirely. That line item becomes their day rate. On a two-day job, that is potentially $1,200 to $1,800 in rental fees you do not pay, and you get the operator's proprietary color workflow as part of the deal.

This is the economic argument for hiring equipped operators over sourcing everything through a rental house. The math favors it on most productions under $50,000 total.

Know the difference between productions that need a full custom rental package and those that are better served by an equipped company. Most Dallas corporate and brand productions fall in the second category.

Plan accordingly. Your budget will thank you.
