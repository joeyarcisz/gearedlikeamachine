The most common question a production company in Dallas hears is "what does a video cost?" The most honest answer is: it depends on what you're actually buying.

That's not a dodge. It's the structure of the industry.

A two-person run-and-gun day with a mirrorless camera and a single light can produce a finished corporate interview for $3,500. A properly staffed commercial shoot for a regional TV campaign can run $85,000 before talent and agency fees. Both are "video production in Dallas." They are not the same product.

Here is how it actually breaks down.

## The Four Budget Tiers

Every production in the DFW market falls into one of four tiers. The tier defines not just price, but what you get on set, what the deliverable can do, and how long the footage will hold up.

### Tier 1: Content-Level Work ($1,500 to $7,500)

This is the range that covers solo operators, run-and-gun shoots, basic talking-head interviews, and social content days. You are hiring one to two people, often a DP doubling as director, sometimes a PA. The camera is usually a cinema-capable mirrorless body. Lighting is minimal. Audio is a lav and a boom if you're lucky.

At this tier, execution speed is the product. You can shoot a testimonial in half a day, turn around a cut in 48 hours, and move on. For internal communications, social posts, and basic website explainers, this works fine.

Where it breaks: product launches, brand campaigns, anything you intend to run as paid media for longer than one quarter.

### Tier 2: Corporate/Branded Production ($7,500 to $35,000)

This is where most mid-market Dallas brands live. A dedicated DP, a producer on prep, a gaffer bringing a proper lighting package, possibly a camera operator as a second pair of hands. Two to three shoot days. Real pre-production. Location scouting, tech scout, a shot list that was built with intention.

The difference between Tier 1 and Tier 2 is not the camera. It's the lighting department. A gaffer with a proper kit transforms what a location can do. Flat corporate offices become usable sets. A loading dock becomes a background with depth. The footage holds up.

A $15,000 brand film at this tier will include: DP day rate ($1,200 to $2,500), camera package ($800 to $2,000/day), gaffer + electric kit ($600 to $1,200/day), production coordinator, location fees, catering, and a post package for editorial, color, and mix.

### Tier 3: Commercial Production ($35,000 to $150,000)

At this level, the shoot is a department-by-department operation. Director, DP, full camera department (operator, 1st AC, 2nd AC), gaffer, best boy, grip department (key grip, dolly grip, swing), art department, hair and makeup, talent fees, production insurance, catering for 15 to 25 people, and a producer who manages all of it.

This is the tier where brands that care about where they appear spend their production dollars. A regional TV spot, a product launch film, a campaign anchor piece. The daily burn rate on a Tier 3 shoot is $12,000 to $25,000, not counting post.

Post at this tier is its own budget line. Color with a dedicated colorist, audio mix and sound design, possible VFX or motion graphics, often a full round of revisions with agency feedback.

### Tier 4: National/Major Campaign ($150,000+)

National broadcast, agency-directed campaigns, anything with a SAG-AFTRA contract for talent. DFW has work at this level, particularly in the automotive, energy, and financial sectors. The production infrastructure is identical to what you'd find in LA or Chicago. Some of it flies in from there.

## What the Money Actually Pays For

Most clients sticker-shock on mid-tier production numbers because they're thinking about camera cost, not crew structure. The camera is a fraction of the budget. Here's where the money goes on a representative $40,000 commercial shoot:

**Crew labor:** $14,000 to $18,000. This covers DP, director (if separate), gaffer, grip, camera operator, 1st AC, hair/makeup, PA. DFW non-union day rates: DPs run $900 to $1,600, gaffers run $500 to $900, camera operators run $500 to $750. Seven crew for two days is $10,000 to $14,000 in labor alone before any overhead.

**Equipment/gear rental:** $4,000 to $8,000. Camera package, lighting package, grip package. A 5-ton grip/lighting truck in Dallas runs $1,500 to $3,500/day.

**Location and permits:** $1,000 to $4,000. Studio rental in DFW runs $500 to $2,500/day. Location fees on private property vary wildly.

**Art direction and props:** $500 to $2,500.

**Catering and craft services:** $500 to $1,500.

**Post-production:** $6,000 to $12,000. Editor, colorist, sound design, music licensing.

**Production overhead and company fee:** $4,000 to $8,000. This is the 20 to 25% markup that covers insurance, project management, pre-production time, revisions management, and the production company's margin.

That math adds up to $30,000 to $54,000, which is why a quality commercial in the DFW market lands in the $35,000 to $85,000 range.

## The Texas Factor

Dallas is not Los Angeles. Rates are lower, which is a feature, not a compromise.

DFW non-union crew rates run 15 to 25% below LA/NYC for equivalent roles. A DP who would bill $2,500/day in LA bills $1,200 to $1,800 in Dallas at the same skill level. The equipment rental market is competitive. Studio day rates are a fraction of what you'd pay in Culver City.

The Texas SB22 film incentive (effective September 2025) adds up to a 31% cash rebate on qualified Texas spending for larger productions. For branded content and commercial work, that incentive does not apply directly, but it reflects the state's commitment to building production infrastructure here.

The practical result: a $40,000 project in DFW is genuinely comparable to a $55,000 project in LA. You're getting the same crew caliber, often with more experience than their rate suggests, in a market where everybody is hungry to do premium work.

## What Makes Bids Different from the Same Production Company

You can get two bids from two reputable Dallas production companies for the same project brief and see a $20,000 difference. The reasons are almost always one of four things:

**Crew structure.** One company is quoting a three-person shoot. The other is quoting eight. Neither may be wrong for the brief, but they are not quoting the same product.

**Gear ownership vs. rental.** A company that owns its camera and lighting package marks it up at near-rental-house rates and keeps the margin. A company that rents everything passes through costs at 15% markup. The camera line on the budget can look similar, but the economics are very different.

**Post scope.** Are revisions included? How many rounds? Is color graded by the editor or a dedicated colorist? Music licensed for how long and what media? The post section of a budget is where scope creep lives.

**Overhead model.** A lean two-person shop billing 25% overhead on a $30,000 hard cost base charges $37,500. A full-service company with staff producers billing 28% charges $38,400. These are close. But if one of them is padding line items to hide overhead, the comparison breaks.

Ask for itemized bids. Compare crew counts, not just totals.

## The Question to Ask Before You Bid Anything

"What problem is this video solving, and how long does it need to solve it?"

A video for a trade show booth this quarter needs to be good. A brand anchor piece that will define your company's visual identity for two years needs to be right. The budget that makes sense for each is not the same.

Production is the only cost in your marketing stack that is permanent. Ad spend disappears when the campaign ends. The footage stays. A $15,000 video that doesn't perform what you needed it to costs you $15,000 plus the next production to fix it.

Budget toward what the footage has to do, not toward what makes the invoice comfortable.
