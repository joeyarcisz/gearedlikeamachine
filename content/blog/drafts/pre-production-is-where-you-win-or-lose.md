The best DPs are not the best camera operators. They are the best planners.

Shooting is the smallest part of the job. The image you capture at 4pm on a Tuesday was decided two weeks earlier in a room with a script, a floor plan, and a conversation with the director. Everything between the plan and the frame is execution. Execution follows preparation.

This is not a productivity argument. This is a quality argument. The most visually stunning films ever made were meticulously planned. **No Country for Old Men** had Roger Deakins walking every location months before a camera showed up. **There Will Be Blood** had Paul Thomas Anderson and Robert Elswit storyboarding the Dillon oil field sequences in obsessive detail. **Children of Men** had Emmanuel Lubezki running through the Bexhill sequence with Alfonso Cuaron over and over until the 7-minute single take lived in both their bodies before it lived on film.

Prep is not overhead. Prep is the work.

## The Shot List: Your Visual Contract

A shot list is not a bureaucratic document. It is a promise you make to yourself and your director: these are the images we will capture, and this is why each one matters.

Most DPs write shot lists reactively, on set, in response to what the location gives them. That is amateur hour. The shot list should be drafted from the script, refined after the tech scout, locked before camera day, and used as the production's north star from call to wrap.

Every line item answers four questions. What is the shot size? What is the camera movement? What is the lens? What is the emotional purpose?

If you cannot answer the fourth question, you do not have a shot. You have a setup.

**Mad Max: Fury Road** is 120 minutes of film built from a 3,500-shot storyboard that George Miller drew by hand before a single frame was shot. The post-apocalyptic chaos on screen is the product of surgical pre-production. The freedom was earned by the rigor.

## The Tech Scout: Your Location Intelligence

You cannot light a room you have never stood in.

The tech scout is the moment where every abstract plan meets physical reality. The gaffer learns the window positions. The key grip assesses what the rigging points will hold. The DP figures out where the sun is at the hour you are scheduled to shoot. The AD writes down the practical obstacles everyone else will pretend do not exist until shoot day.

Go early. Go at the same time of day you will be shooting. Bring a compass. Bring a light meter. Bring your gaffer. The tech scout without your gaffer is a walk. The tech scout with your gaffer is a planning session.

What you are solving at the tech scout:

**Direction of natural light.** Where does the sun land at your call time? At your scheduled scene time? Which windows are assets and which are problems?

**Power.** Where are the panels? What can they support? What are you trucking in versus pulling from the building?

**Ingress.** How does the equipment move through the space? Can the 12x12 rig fit through the service entrance? Where does the camera truck park?

**The fallback.** What does the plan become if weather turns, if the location changes, if your lead is late? Good scouts produce a plan and an alternate plan.

The tech scout is not a formality. It is the most valuable two hours in your production calendar.

## The Storyboard: Thinking on Paper

Storyboards make you commit.

When an idea lives only in your head, it is protected from scrutiny. The moment you draw it, it has to survive the page. Bad ideas look bad on paper. Weak compositions reveal themselves before you waste a company move on them.

You do not need to be an artist. Rough boxes with stick figures and arrows do the job. The point is not visual beauty. The point is forcing yourself to work out the spatial problem before the clock is running.

There is a hierarchy of storyboard value. The most useful frames are the ones for technically complex shots: crane moves, underwater work, multi-camera action sequences, anything where the blocking of camera and talent must be choreographed to the frame. For dialogue coverage and standard setups, a shot list does the job. Save storyboards for the shots that could go catastrophically wrong without one.

**Kubrick** storyboarded everything. **Spielberg** sketches key sequences by hand. **David Fincher** boards his films so precisely that editors joke there is no coverage because he shot exactly what he planned and nothing else. Fincher's discipline is not a personality quirk. It is a system that eliminates waste.

## Blocking Diagrams: The Director's Floor Plan

Most DPs skip blocking diagrams. Most DPs also spend the first hour of every shooting day solving problems they could have solved the week before.

A blocking diagram is a top-down floor plan of the set with actor positions, camera positions, and movement paths marked in sequence. It looks like a football play. It functions like one too. Everyone knows where they are going before the ball snaps.

The blocking diagram answers questions before they become arguments. Where does the actor hit their mark for the close-up? Where is the camera to catch the over-the-shoulder without seeing the A-camera operator? When the talent walks left to right across the scene, where does camera cross to maintain screen direction?

Work out the geometry on paper. Not on set. Not on the clock.

For multi-camera setups, interview scenarios, or any scene with physical movement from the talent, a blocking diagram is not optional. It is the pre-production document that keeps your shoot day from becoming a negotiation.

## The Script Breakdown: Pre-Visualizing the Invisible

Before you can design images, you have to understand the script as a visual document.

Break it down scene by scene. Tag every location. Mark every transition. Note every practical effect, every special lighting requirement, every moment where the emotional temperature of the scene demands a specific visual approach.

The questions you are answering in the breakdown:

Does this scene read as interior day, interior night, exterior dawn? The answer changes your lighting package.

Does this scene have motivated practical sources? Windows, screens, fire, practicals. If yes, your lighting supports and shapes what is already there. If no, you are building from zero.

Does this scene have camera movement scripted into the visual language of the story? Or is it coverage?

What is the emotional arc of this scene, and which visual tools support that arc? Color temperature shift? A slow push-in? A shift from shallow to deep focus as the character arrives at clarity?

The breakdown turns a screenplay into a production plan. Without it, you are reading the script as a reader. With it, you are reading it as a DP.

## Preparation Is a Competitive Advantage

The director you want to work with has options. They will work with the DP who shows up to the first meeting with visual references, a location strategy, and a point of view on how the story should be told.

Not the one who says, "Let's see what the space gives us."

That phrase sounds like creative flexibility. What it actually communicates is that you have not done the work.

The DPs who get hired for the best projects are the ones who make directors feel safe. Safe means: this person has thought about this more than I have. Safe means: we are not going to lose the day because no one planned the lighting transition. Safe means: when something goes wrong on set, and it always does, this person already has the fallback in their head.

**Gordon Willis** was called "The Prince of Darkness" not because of his personality. It was because his images were darker than anyone else dared to go. He could go that dark because he had worked out every frame in his head before he touched a camera. The darkness was planned. The planning made it possible.

Prep is invisible on screen. But it is responsible for everything you see.

The shot list, the tech scout, the storyboard, the blocking diagram, the script breakdown. Five tools. All used before camera day. All responsible for the quality of every image you capture after it.

The film is made in pre-production. Everything else is just recording it.
