The best interview lighting you will ever produce comes from one source. Not because you lack a second light. Because you chose not to use one.

That choice is the discipline. It forces every element of the setup to justify its existence. The key light, the negative fill, the background distance. Three decisions. Maximum result.

## Why One Light

The standard three-point setup, key, fill, back, produces a result that looks lit. It is even, functional, and reads as production. Broadcast news. Corporate training videos. The light fills every corner of the face and the audience thinks nothing of it, because there is nothing to think about.

One source produces an image with an opinion about the subject. The bright side and the shadow side. The direction light comes from. The depth that the shadow creates across the face. The audience does not consciously register this, but they feel it. The subject becomes dimensional. The frame has a point of view.

Every documentary that has aged well was lit this way. **Errol Morris** builds entire careers on it. His interviewees in **The Fog of War** and **The Act of Killing** are not bathed in light. They are shaped by it. One source, offset, shadow doing half the work the words do.

That is the goal.

## The Four Elements of the Setup

This is not three-point lighting with one of the points removed. It is its own system. Four decisions, made in order.

### 1. The Key: Quality and Angle

The key light is a soft source. A Skypanel through diffusion. A 2x3 Chimera on an M18. A Litepanel Gemini with a fabric diffuser. The actual fixture matters less than the size of the apparent source relative to the subject's face.

Softness determines shadow edge. A softer source produces a gentler transition from lit to shadow. A harder source produces a sharper falloff. For most interview work, the goal is a transition that is noticeable but not theatrical. The face should feel lit from one direction. The shadow should read as a shadow, not a wall.

Position the key at roughly 45 degrees to the side and slightly above eye level. This produces a loop pattern: a small shadow from the nose falling toward the corner of the mouth, the cheek catching light cleanly, the far side beginning to fall off toward shadow.

The alternative is a Rembrandt pattern: key moved further to the side until the nose shadow connects with the cheek shadow, forming a small triangle of light on the unlit side beneath the eye. More dramatic. More character. Use it when the subject and context warrant it. An executive profile. A documentary subject with a complicated story. Not a product testimonial.

The key should be one stop above ambient. Not three. One. That ratio separates the subject from the environment without declaring war on it.

### 2. The Negative Fill: Where Contrast Lives

Most DPs think lighting is about adding light. The one-light setup clarifies that contrast is about subtracting it.

On the shadow side of the subject, place a black V-flat, a piece of duvetyne, or the black side of a floppy. This material absorbs the ambient light that would otherwise wrap around the subject's face and lift the shadow side. Without it, room bounce, ceiling spill, and reflected key light all accumulate on the shadow side. The shadows fill and soften. The contrast ratio collapses. The image goes flat.

The negative fill deepens the shadow side by preventing that accumulation. You are not adding darkness. You are protecting the darkness you already have.

How close you place the negative fill determines how aggressive the effect is. Closer to the subject, more absorption, deeper shadow. Further away, the effect decreases. On most interview setups, the V-flat sits two to three feet from the subject on the shadow side, just out of frame. The result is a falloff ratio that the eye reads as three-dimensional.

**Fincher** uses this on every interview sequence in his documentaries. The subjects in **The Killer** and across his body of commercial work have shadow sides that hold. They do not fill. That depth is not a lens decision or a grade decision. It is a negative fill decision made on set before the camera rolled.

### 3. The Background: Distance Is the Decision

The background in an interview setup is not decoration. It is a tonal and spatial decision that tells the audience how much to trust the frame.

The single most important thing you can do for background depth is distance. Pull the subject away from the background. Not two feet. Six feet minimum. Ten is better. The key light, which is positioned for the subject's face, will fall off rapidly before it reaches the background. This creates natural separation without a dedicated background light.

The inverse square law does this work for you if you let it. Double the distance, quarter the intensity. A key light metered at the subject's face at f/4 will produce roughly f/1 at a background ten feet further away. That two-and-a-half-stop difference is everything. The subject is bright. The background falls into its own tonal value. The image has depth without complexity.

If you cannot pull the subject off the background due to space constraints, the flag becomes essential. A cutter positioned between the key light and the background, cutting the spill, creates the tonal difference you need. The subject is lit. The wall is not.

**Werner Herzog** in every interview he has ever shot as director or subject demonstrates this without trying. The subjects feel like they exist in their own pool of light, regardless of location. The background recedes. The face advances. Distance and negative fill do it every time.

### 4. The Eye: The Catch Light Test

Every setup is complete or incomplete based on one thing: the catch light in the eye.

The catch light is the reflection of your key source in the subject's iris. A single white shape, positioned in the upper half of the eye. This is what makes eyes appear alive rather than flat.

If the catch light is missing, the key is too far off-axis. Move it forward or raise it. If there are two catch lights, a fill lamp crept into the setup. Eliminate or move it. If the catch light is in the lower half of the eye, the key is below eye level. Raise it.

One catch light. Upper portion of the eye. That is the test. Everything else in the setup can be right and wrong simultaneously, but if this fails, the subject looks dead.

## The Full Setup

One soft key light, 45 degrees off-axis, slightly above eye level. One negative fill V-flat on the shadow side. Subject pulled at least six feet from the background. Camera metered to make the key the dominant source.

That is it.

The total hardware: one light, one stand, one diffusion frame or chimera, one V-flat. You can pack this in a single van. You can set it in twenty minutes. You can use it in a boardroom, a warehouse, an outdoor location, a living room. The variables change. The system does not.

**Errol Morris's Interrotron setups** in **The Fog of War** are a more sophisticated version of this same principle. One motivated source per subject. The depth in those interviews comes from the ratio and the negative fill, not from a complex rig. Robert McNamara looks like the weight of history is on him. That is a one-light decision.

## Ambient Is the Enemy

The setup breaks whenever you let ambient light become uncontrolled.

Walk into any location and the ambient already exists: window light, overhead fluorescents, practical lamps, spill from adjacent rooms. All of it is contributing to the shadow side of your subject's face before your key is even powered on.

The sequence matters. Kill the ambient first. Turn off overheads. Flag the windows or time the setup to avoid direct sun spill. Identify every source contributing to the shadow side and eliminate or reduce it. Then power on your key.

If you set up your key before controlling the ambient, you are building on an unstable foundation. The ambient will fight your key for the shadow side and win.

The goal is total control of the light in the frame. One source on the bright side. Controlled darkness on the shadow side. Everything else eliminated.

## When to Add a Second Light

The only second light that earns a place in this setup is a hair light or rim light, and only in specific circumstances: when the subject's hair or clothing is close in value to the background and the eye cannot separate them tonally.

A rim light placed slightly behind and above the subject, pointed at the back of the head and shoulder, creates an edge separation. It is not meant to be seen as a light. It is meant to be an outline. Barely there. One-half to one stop below key exposure on the shoulder.

This is not a three-point setup. It is a one-light setup with a separation tool. The difference matters. The rim is there to solve a specific technical problem. If the subject already separates from the background tonally, the rim light is excess.

When you add the second light without a specific reason, you are doing three-point lighting. When you add it to solve a defined problem, you are still doing one-light work.

## What the Setup Teaches

Every setup choice is a distillation of craft. The one-light interview forces you to understand exactly what each decision does because there are only four decisions to make. There is no fill light to blame when the contrast ratio is wrong. There is no backlight to apologize for when the background does not separate. There is no third source to credit when the image has depth.

The quality of the image is entirely traceable to the quality of the four decisions: key quality, negative fill proximity, background distance, and catch light position.

When you can produce a compelling, dimensional, purposeful interview setup from one light consistently, you understand light. Not as a fixture choice. As a system.

The DP who walks in with one Skypanel and a V-flat and produces a better image than the crew that just unloaded six fixtures is not lucky. They know something that six-light crew does not.

The setup is not the limitation. It is the test.
