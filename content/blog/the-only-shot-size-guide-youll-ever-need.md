Films are made of sequences. Sequences are made of scenes. Scenes are made of shots. Every shot choice shapes rhythm, tone, and meaning.

Shot selection should be the highest priority for both a director and a DP. The wrong shot at the wrong moment kills a scene. The right one makes it unforgettable.

Here are the nine shot sizes that make up every film ever made, from Blade Runner to Coco.

## 1. Establishing Shot (ES)

**The World Builder.** Wide enough to establish geography, time of day, and the scale of subjects in their environment. The most common way to open a scene or an entire film.

**Use when:** Scene opener, location transition, world-building, establishing time of day.

**Blade Runner 2049** opens with establishing shots of industrial farms. First impression of near-future Earth.

## 2. Master Shot (MS*)

**The Safety Net.** Captures the entire scene playing out. Confirms who is in it, where they are, and their spatial relationships. The editor's fallback.

**Use when:** Full scene coverage, group dynamics, editor safety.

**The Godfather Part II** plays the family dinner in the master to emphasize closeness. Then Michael sits alone and the emptiness hits.

## 3. Wide Shot (WS)

**The Scale Statement.** Subject far from camera. Not about location (that is establishing), it is about scale. Makes subjects feel small, lost, overwhelmed, or isolated.

**Use when:** Isolation, loneliness, environment scale.

**Phantom Thread** frames Alma and Reynolds dwarfed by a messy ballroom. Together, yet isolated from the world.

> The establishing shot says "here is where we are." The wide shot says "here is how small we are inside it."

## 4. Full Shot (FS)

**Head to Toe.** Subject fills the frame from top to bottom. Tight enough for facial storytelling but wide enough for full body, posture, and wardrobe.

**Use when:** Physicality, wardrobe, character entrance.

## 5. Medium Full / Cowboy (MFS)

**The Power Stance.** Head to just below the waist. Named for gun holster height. Reads as confident, dangerous, confrontational. The standoff framing.

**Use when:** Confrontation, power, weapons drawn.

**The Favourite** uses cowboy framing on Lady Sarah with a firearm. Not a western, still unmistakably a standoff.

## 6. Medium Shot (MS)

**The Workhorse.** The most popular shot in cinema. Waist to above the head. Similar to how we see people in conversation. Neutral: neither dramatic nor distancing.

**Use when:** Dialogue, neutral ground, eyes + body.

**Coco** captures Miguel's medium shot with his shrine, the TV, and his reaction all in one intimate frame.

> The medium shot is cinema's default because it mirrors how humans actually see each other. It is the handshake distance.

## 7. Medium Close-Up (MCU)

**The Focus Pull.** Chest to above the head. Strips away distraction. Close enough for emotion, wide enough to include hands, props, or actions at chest level.

**Use when:** Reducing distraction, character + prop, reaction.

**Avengers: Endgame** frames Thanos's snap in an MCU. Both the gauntlet and his face. When he fails, we see it all.

## 8. Close-Up (CU)

**The Empathy Engine.** The most powerful visual weapon for emotion and dramatic beats. Typically at eye level. Front row seat for thoughts and feelings. This is about empathy.

**Use when:** Emotional turn, decision moment, anxiety, empathy.

**The Shining** gives us "Here's Johnny." The close-up as cinema's most devastating weapon.

## 9. Extreme Close-Up (ECU)

**The Scalpel.** Isolates a specific area: eyes, lips, a prop. The most intimate, dramatic, and potentially startling shot size. Maximum emphasis. Use deliberately.

**Use when:** Maximum emphasis, crucial detail, startling reveal.

**Kill Bill Vol. 1** shows The Bride's frantic eyes as the Crazy 88 swarm. Pure adrenaline through the eyes alone.

## The Spectrum: Distance to Intimacy

Every shot lives on a scale. One end is maximum distance and context. The other end is maximum intimacy and emotion. Moving along it shifts the audience's relationship to the subject.

**Context Zone** (ES, Master, WS): Where are we? Who is here? What is the scale? The orientation shots.

**Engagement Zone** (FS, MFS, MS): Conversational range. Body language, posture, wardrobe. Where most runtime lives.

**Intimacy Zone** (MCU, CU, ECU): Emotion, revelation, emphasis. The audience enters the character's inner world.

## The Cheat Sheet

- **ES** - Establishing. Geography, time, world. Scene opener.
- **MS*** - Master. Full scene coverage. Editor's safety net.
- **WS** - Wide. Subject scale vs environment. Isolation.
- **FS** - Full. Head to toe. Physicality and wardrobe.
- **MFS** - Cowboy. Head to mid-thigh. Power. Confrontation.
- **MS** - Medium. Waist up. The workhorse. Conversational.
- **MCU** - Med Close-Up. Chest up. Focus. Character + props.
- **CU** - Close-Up. Face. The empathy engine. Emotion.
- **ECU** - Extreme CU. Eyes or detail. The scalpel. Max emphasis.

Nine shots. That is every tool you need. The rest is knowing when to use which one.
