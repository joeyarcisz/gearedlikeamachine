# Cardboard Spaceship — Meeting Action Plan

There's a specific job for one of their clients. The deck is the project brief. Savannah and Michael both already know you can deliver. You've produced together before on a Cardboard Spaceship internal project. The only thing that's new here is the client layer. This is the first time they'd be putting you on a project where there's an end client involved, with brand guidelines, approval chains, client presence on set, and Cardboard Spaceship's reputation on the line with that client.

Your job is not to convince them you're talented. They know that. Your job is to make them feel completely comfortable that you can operate in a client production environment where the stakes and the politics are different from a self-funded project.

---

## Before the Meeting: Study the Deck

When the deck arrives, read it twice.

**First pass: absorb.** What's the project? Who's the client? What are the deliverables? How many shoot days? What's the creative direction? What's the timeline?

**Second pass: think about the client.** What does this client care about? What's their brand identity? What would make them nervous on set? What does "success" look like from the client's perspective, not just the creative perspective? This is the thinking that separates someone who's done client work from someone who hasn't.

**Come in with:**
- A clear understanding of the project scope and what the client is trying to achieve
- 2-3 specific observations that show you thought about the work, not just read the brief
- Questions that demonstrate you understand the client production environment

---

## Phase 1: The Open

You all know each other. You've worked together. Keep this short and natural.

This isn't a first impression meeting. It's three people who've been in the field together sitting down to talk about the next one. Match their energy. Savannah and Michael are calm and laid back, so be that. Quick check-ins, then straight to the project.

---

## Phase 2: The Project Discussion

They'll walk through the brief. Engage with it. The questions you ask here are how you demonstrate that you understand what changes when a client is in the picture.

### Ask Questions That Show Client-Environment Awareness

This is the gap you're closing. Not "can Joey shoot?" but "does Joey know how to operate when there's a brand, an agency, and client stakeholders involved?"

**Creative process questions:**
- "How locked is the creative? Has the client signed off on the concept, or is there still room for it to shift?"
- "Are there brand guidelines I should review before I start thinking about the visual approach?"
- "Is there a director, or is Cardboard Spaceship directing? I want to know who's owning the creative vision on set so I'm supporting that, not working against it."

**Client dynamics questions:**
- "Will the client be on set? If so, how do you prefer to manage that? I'm used to keeping my communication running through the director and producer so the client isn't getting conflicting input."
- "What does the approval flow look like? Are we reviewing on a monitor on set, or does everything go through post for client review?"
- "Is there an agency layer between you and the client, or is Cardboard Spaceship the direct relationship?"

**Operational questions:**
- "What does the crew look like? Am I bringing my department or are you crewing separately?"
- "What's the post pipeline? Who's editing, who's coloring? I want to make sure I'm shooting in a way that sets them up."
- "What's the deliverables list? Knowing the final formats shapes how I approach coverage."
- "Is there budget for a prep day? On client work I like to have time to walk locations, finalize the shot list, and make sure there are no surprises on the day."

These questions don't just gather information. They signal to Savannah and Michael that you've done this before and you understand how a client production differs from what the three of you did together last time.

### Naturally Reference Your Client Work Experience

You've shot for SpaceX, ESPN, Ford, U.S. Polo Assn., Coca-Cola. These are major brands with real stakeholders, agency oversight, and brand standards. You've operated in exactly the environment they need you to operate in.

**Don't make a list of clients.** They know your portfolio. Instead, reference experience when it's contextually relevant:

- If the project involves a specific challenge (tight client approval process, on-set client presence, brand restrictions), mention how you've navigated that before. "On the Ford project through Hogarth, the client had a rep on set reviewing every setup. I made sure to loop them in at the monitor so they felt included without slowing us down."
- If the creative approach requires something specific, reference a project where you executed something similar. One mention, specific, relevant.

The goal isn't to prove your portfolio. They've seen it. The goal is to connect your experience to the specific demands of their client project so they can picture you in that environment.

---

## Phase 3: Show You Understand the Difference

The previous project you did together was Cardboard Spaceship's own. They owned the creative, the footage, the outcome. Nobody had to answer to a brand manager or present dailies to an agency.

Without saying any of this explicitly, demonstrate through your conversation that you understand what changes:

**Chain of command.** On the internal project, decisions could be made in the moment between the three of you. On a client project, there's an approval structure. Show that you respect it and know how to work within it without slowing production down.

**Protecting their client relationship.** Cardboard Spaceship sold this project to their client. Their reputation is attached to the outcome. Show that you understand your role is to make Cardboard Spaceship look great to their client, not to make yourself look great.

**Deliverable precision.** Internal projects have creative flexibility. Client projects have specific deliverables with specific specs and specific deadlines. Show that you're precise about what needs to be captured and delivered.

**Set behavior.** On an internal project, the set is loose. On a client project, professionalism is constant because you never know who's watching or what gets reported back. You already know this from your client work, but it's worth signaling that you operate that way.

**None of this needs to be a speech.** It comes through in your questions, your reactions, and how you talk about the project. If you ask about the approval flow, they know you've thought about it. If you mention keeping communication through the director and producer when a client is on set, they know you've been in that situation and handled it well.

---

## Phase 4: If Rates or Budget Come Up

These are people you've worked with. Be direct.

**If they ask for your rate:**
"For DP with my camera package, I'm at [day rate] plus [gear rate]. If you need me to bring crew or take on more of the production scope, let me look at the full brief and come back with a number."

**If they share the budget:**
Listen. If it works, say it works. If it's tight, say "let me see how to make that work within the scope" and follow up with a plan. Don't negotiate in this meeting.

**If they don't bring it up:**
Don't force it. Let the project conversation be the focus. Rates can happen in a follow-up once the scope is clear.

**If they reference the old project's rate:**
"That was a different scope. Let me look at what this one needs and I'll come back with something that makes sense."

---

## Phase 5: The Close

This should feel like the natural next step, not a pitch.

### Lock Down

1. **What they need from you to move forward.** "What do you need from me? Gear list, crew recommendation, anything else?" This signals you're already in execution mode.

2. **Hold the dates.** "When's the shoot? I'll block it off." Simple. Committed.

3. **Production coordination.** "Once we're moving, am I coordinating with Michael, Savannah, or both?" Shows you're thinking about workflow.

### The Tone

Peers getting ready to do a project together. Not a vendor thanking someone for an opportunity. You've already worked with these people. The close should feel like "alright, let's do this" not "thank you for considering me."

---

## Phase 6: Immediately After

Within 30 minutes:

1. **Write down every project detail discussed.** Client, deliverables, timeline, locations, crew needs, creative direction, budget signals.
2. **Assess: what's your role?** DP only, DP + gear, DP + crew, or broader production scope?
3. **Note any client-specific requirements** that will affect how you prep or shoot.
4. **Drop the notes here.** I'll help scope the response and draft the follow-up.

---

## Conversation Landmines

**If the scope is bigger than expected:**
"That's a bigger production. Let me think through the crew and logistics and I'll come back with how I'd put it together."

**If the timeline is aggressive:**
"That's tight but doable if we lock pre-production by [date]. What's the client's approval timeline look like?"

**If there's a director and your role is DP:**
Embrace it fully. "Who's directing? I'd want to connect with them before the shoot to get on the same page visually." Don't fight for creative ownership on a client job where a director is attached.

**If there's no director and they want you to direct and DP:**
"I can handle both. The prep time changes, though. I'd want an extra day to develop the shot approach so we're not figuring things out when the client is standing there."

**If they mention other people they're considering:**
Don't compete. Don't ask who. Just keep being the person who's already thinking about how to execute this project. Your track record with them is your advantage, not your pitch.

---

## The One Thing to Remember

They already trust your talent. They already trust you personally. The only question is whether you can take what the three of you did together on their internal project and do it with a client watching. You've done exactly that for brands that are as big or bigger than whoever their client is. Let that show through your questions and your instincts, not through a sales pitch. The moment they stop thinking about whether you can handle the client environment and start thinking about the project itself, you've got the job.
