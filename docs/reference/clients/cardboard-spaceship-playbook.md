## What to Watch For in the Deck

### Determine Your Role Immediately

This is the most important read. Are they looking for:

1. **A DP for hire** — They direct, they produce, they need a shooter. Joey shows up with a camera package and executes. Day rate model. Service work.
2. **A production subcontractor** — They sell the project to their client, then hand off production to GLM. GLM handles crew, gear, logistics, shoot. Cardboard Spaceship handles client relationship, creative direction, post (maybe). Project fee model.
3. **A creative partner** — They want Joey's creative voice, not just execution. Collaborative on concept, treatment, look. This is the premium position.
4. **An ongoing vendor on a roster** — They want GLM on their preferred vendor list for ongoing projects. Relationship play, not a single job.

**How to read the signals:**
- If the deck mentions "crew" or "camera department" — they see you as a DP for hire
- If the deck mentions "production partner" or "production vendor" — subcontractor role
- If they reference your reel or aesthetic specifically — creative partner potential
- If the deck is a general capabilities overview with no specific project — roster play

### Read Service Work vs. Creative Work

From the business practices reference (Wandering DP Ep. #477): Misreading which type of job you're on creates the most friction. If Cardboard Spaceship has a fully formed creative vision and needs execution, pushing your creative agenda creates problems. If they want creative input, being passive is a missed opportunity.

### Watch the Non-Circumvent Scope

The NDA you signed has a non-circumvent clause. Pay attention to:
- Which clients they name in the deck — these are now covered parties
- How broadly they define the relationship scope
- Whether they expect exclusivity or just non-circumvention on introduced parties
- The pre-existing relationships carve-out you negotiated protects any clients you already know independently

### Watch for Rate Expectations

- Do they mention budget ranges for projects?
- Do they ask for your rate card?
- Do they reference "competitive rates" or "market rates" (code for: we want you cheap)?
- Do they position themselves as the premium option to clients but expect vendor discounts from you?

## Questions Joey Should Ask (or Listen For Answers To)

### About the Relationship

1. "What does a typical engagement look like when you bring on a production partner?"
2. "How do you usually structure the working relationship — do you direct on set, or do you hand off production?"
3. "Who's the point of contact during production — Savannah, or does it change per project?"
4. "How many production partners do you typically work with, or are you looking for a primary?"

### About the Work

5. "What types of projects do you see coming up in the next 3-6 months?"
6. "What's the typical timeline from brief to delivery on your projects?"
7. "Do you handle post in-house, or is that part of what you'd need from us?"
8. "What does the creative approval chain look like — does the client review on set, or do you buffer that?"

### About Business Terms

9. "How do you typically structure payment with production partners?"
10. "What's your standard turnaround on payment after delivery?"
11. "Are projects usually one-offs, or do you work on retainer with your production partners?"

### DO NOT Ask Yet

- Do not ask about specific rates in the first meeting. Let them bring it up
- Do not volunteer your rate floor ($1,200/day) or target ($2,500/day) unprompted
- Do not ask about portfolio usage rights yet — this is in the NDA and can be addressed per-project later

## How to Position GLM

### Lead With

- **National-caliber portfolio** — SpaceX, ESPN, Ford, Coca-Cola, U.S. Polo Assn. in a market where most production partners top out at regional brands
- **Full-stack capability** — Concept through delivery under one roof. Not just a camera operator, but a production company that can scale
- **Equipment ownership** — Own the camera package (RED V-Raptor 8K, RED Komodo-X, etc.), which means no third-party rental coordination and faster deployment
- **Operator network** — Deep bench of vetted crew, not locked into one team. Assemble the exact team each project demands

### Do NOT Lead With

- Price. Never compete on price with this client roster
- Desperation or over-eagerness. This is a mutual evaluation, not a job interview
- Technical specs or gear lists unless asked. Lead with outcomes and portfolio, not equipment

### Positioning Framework

GLM's value proposition to Cardboard Spaceship: **"You get a production company with a national-caliber reel, owned equipment, and a deep crew network, at DFW overhead, not LA/NYC overhead."**

This is the sweet spot. Their clients (Google, IBM, Mercedes-Benz) expect premium quality. GLM delivers premium quality at a price point that gives Cardboard Spaceship healthy margin on the subcontract.

## Pricing Strategy If Rates Come Up

### If They Ask for a Day Rate (DP Role)

- **Floor:** $1,200/day (never go below this)
- **Target:** $2,000-$2,500/day for agency commercial work
- **Plus gear rental:** Market rate for owned equipment (RED V-Raptor package: $1,500-$2,500/day)
- Frame it as: "My day rate is [X], and the camera package runs [Y]/day. That's all-in for DP + cinema camera + lenses."

### If They Ask for Project-Based Pricing (Production Subcontractor Role)

- Do not quote on the spot. Take the brief, scope it properly, come back with a number
- Use the Good/Better/Best framework from the pricing models doc
- Their typical project range is $50K-$250K for named clients. GLM's subcontract portion would be a fraction of that
- Realistic subcontract range for production services on a $100K project: $15K-$40K depending on scope, shoot days, crew size, and equipment

### If They Mention Retainer

- This is the premium scenario. Monthly retainer = recurring revenue
- Retainer tiers range from $2K-$30K+/month
- For a starting relationship: a small retainer ($3K-$5K/month) guaranteeing priority availability + discounted project rates could work
- Do not propose this yourself. If they mention it, explore it

## Red Flags to Watch For

- They expect premium execution but want budget rates
- They want to own all creative decisions but expect you to carry all production risk
- Vague scope with phrases like "we'll figure it out on the day"
- No mention of prep time or pre-production budget
- Pressure to commit to rates before seeing any project details
- Expectation that you'll work on spec before a project is awarded
- Language about "exposure" or "getting your name out there" as compensation supplements

## Green Flags

- They reference specific projects with defined scope
- They ask about your process, not just your price
- They mention prep days and pre-production as part of the budget
- They talk about ongoing/repeat work, not just a one-off
- They have a clear creative approval chain and client management process
- They're transparent about their margin structure (rare but ideal)
- Savannah's role as "Associate Producer and Content Manager" suggests they have organized production infrastructure
