## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Key takeaways for Joey's specific situation:**

### Where You Stand Right Now
You're coming in as an owner/operator director — someone who can DP and direct simultaneously. This is simultaneously your biggest advantage and the single biggest positioning challenge you'll face. The industry respects competence and track record above almost everything else. Your 20+ years of DP experience, national brand credits (U.S. Polo Assn., SpaceX Starlink, Troy Aikman Super Bowl spot), and equipment ownership give you a credibility floor most emerging directors don't have.

### The Hard Numbers (What to Charge)
- **Direct-to-brand corporate and commercial directing (your near-term sweet spot):** $2,500–$5,000/shoot day as director + separate DP/camera package rate, or an all-in owner/operator day rate of $3,500–$7,500/day
- **Regional TV commercials:** $3,500–$8,000/shoot day for director (non-union, direct-to-brand or regional agency)
- **National TV commercial aspiration:** $7,500–$15,000/shoot day for mid-tier/established directors; emerging directors can break in at $5,000–$10,000/day with the right project
- **The 10% rule:** Many directors in the $75K–$200K project budget range charge 8–12% of total project budget as their all-in creative/director fee
- **DGA commercial minimum (2024–2025):** $1,779/day — this is your absolute floor if working union; most working directors are 3x–15x above this

### What You Need to Do First
1. Build a dedicated directing reel separate from your DP reel — the industry evaluates them differently
2. Get at least 3–5 strong commercial directing credits (can be spec, pro bono national brand, or existing work reframed as directed pieces)
3. Develop your treatment writing process — this is the #1 skill gap for most transitioning DPs
4. Start bidding directly for regional and brand-direct work now; don't wait for a rep
5. Price your directing services separately from your DP services from day one

### The Market Reality (2025–2026)
The commercial production industry has contracted significantly since 2021–2022. Brands are doing more in-house, agencies have shrunk, and fewer boards are going out. This actually benefits owner/operator directors like Joey — leaner budgets favor people who can wear multiple hats without compromising quality. The regional/direct-to-brand market is where the volume is right now, and it's very accessible from DFW.

## 3. FEE STRUCTURE BREAKDOWN

Commercial directors don't just charge a shoot day rate. The total director compensation package is structured across multiple phases.

### 3A. The Core Fee Components

**1. Director's Creative Fee / Prep Fee (Pre-Production)**
- Covers the director's time from treatment writing through pre-pro meeting
- Includes: treatment development, creative calls, chemistry meetings, creative concepting, shot list/board development, location scouting oversight, casting involvement, look book development
- **Typical range:**
  - National/agency work: $2,500–$10,000+ (separate from shoot days, often billed as prep days at reduced rate)
  - Mid-tier independent: 2–4 prep days at 50–75% of shoot day rate
  - All-in model: lumped into total project creative fee

**2. Shoot Day Rate**
- The primary revenue line; per actual production day
- Typically the highest per-day rate in the fee structure
- Overtime kicks in at 12 hours; double-day at 18 hours (DGA)

**3. Travel Days**
- Travel days typically billed at 50–100% of shoot day rate
- Standard: 1 travel day each direction
- Per diem: industry standard $75–$150/day for meals when on location
- Airfare and hotel always covered by production

**4. Post-Production / Editorial Oversight Days**
- Typically billed at 50–75% of shoot day rate
- Common on brand films and national commercials; less common on straight corporate work
- Timeline: usually 2–5 days of oversight depending on project complexity

**5. Director's Cut Fee**
- A specific fee for delivering a director's cut before the agency/client cut
- Often bundled into total creative fee rather than separate line item
- On DGA commercial jobs: director's right to deliver a cut is contractually protected

**6. All-In Creative Fee Model (Flat Project Fee)**
- Most common structure for regional, direct-to-brand, and mid-tier work
- Covers: prep, shoot, limited post oversight, creative fee — all in one number
- **Formula:** 8–12% of total project production budget
- Example: $100K project budget = $8,000–$12,000 director fee
- Example: $200K project budget = $16,000–$24,000 director fee
- A UK commercial director confirmed publicly: 10% of budget, projects in the £60K–£150K range

**7. Percentage-of-Budget Models**
- 8–12% for directors in the $75K–$500K budget range
- Lower % (5–8%) for very large budgets ($500K+)
- Higher % (12–20%) for smaller budgets ($10K–$50K)
- **Joey's recommended pricing:**
  - Projects under $25K: 15–20% director fee minimum or flat $2,500
  - Projects $25K–$75K: 12–15%
  - Projects $75K–$200K: 10–12%
  - Projects $200K+: 8–10%

**8. Buyout vs. Residual Structures**
- **Buyout:** One-time fee covers all usage in perpetuity. Standard for non-union/direct-to-brand.
- **Residuals/Usage-based:** Ongoing compensation tied to how/where content runs. More common on DGA union national TV work.
- **Usage impacts the fee:** National broadcast = more than internal-only. Build usage scope into every contract.

**9. Agency vs. Direct-to-Brand Differences**
- **Through agency:** Higher total budgets; more process overhead; more creative alignment meetings
- **Direct-to-brand:** Lower total budgets typical; faster decisions; more creative control; repeat business more likely
- **Fee difference:** Direct-to-brand directors can charge 10–20% more per project because no agency layer extracting markup

## 4. THE TREATMENT/PITCH PROCESS EXPLAINED

### What Is a Director's Treatment?
A director's treatment is a document (and often visual presentation) that explains how the director intends to execute the commercial concept. It is the director's creative proposal, competitive pitch, and visual pitch deck combined.

**A treatment typically includes:**
- Director's vision statement — their read on the brief; what they bring emotionally and conceptually
- Reference imagery / look book — visual references defining tone, color palette, lighting, location aesthetic
- Scene-by-scene breakdown — how the spot plays out beat by beat from the director's POV
- Cinematography approach — lens choices, camera movement, format, framerate
- Talent approach — how they'll cast and direct talent
- Music and sound direction — mood references, style, temp track idea
- Post and color direction — broad strokes on look development

### When Is a Treatment Required?
- Any job bidding competitively (triple-bid process)
- Most national TV commercial bids
- Most brand film bids
- Many larger digital campaign bids
- **NOT required for:** Direct-to-brand repeat client work, small-budget corporate, some direct-hire regional shoots

### Turnaround Time
- **Standard:** 5–7 business days from brief receipt
- **Rush (large national bid):** Sometimes 3–5 days

### Do Directors Charge for Treatments?
- **Almost never.** Treatments are free bids — part of the pitch/competition process.
- Only if asked to do unusual amounts of development work might a creative development fee be discussed
- **Win rates:** A working director might pitch 1 in 3 to 1 in 5 jobs. At the emerging stage, win rates are lower.
- Experienced directors confirmed: initial agency lists of 10+ get narrowed to 3 who actually bid

### Treatment Writing Best Practices
- Invest in great visual sourcing (Pinterest, Behance, film stills, photography)
- Write your director's statement in first person, voice forward — personality shows here
- Keep it focused; a tight 8–12 page treatment beats a rambling 25-pager
- End with a clear production approach showing you understand the practical reality
- Show you've studied the BRAND, not just the script

## 5. PRE-PRODUCTION WORKFLOW

### Phase 1: The Bid / Creative Call (Before Award)

**Creative Call / Chemistry Meeting**
- 30 min to 1 hour video call with agency creative team (ACD, CD, copywriter, art director)
- Purpose: agency gauges interpretation, personality, collaborative instincts
- Typically only 3 directors are invited to creative calls from an initial shortlist of 10+

**Treatment Submission**
- Director submits treatment; production company submits budget/bid simultaneously
- Agency selects a "recommend" (preferred director) for client approval

### Phase 2: Post-Award / Pre-Production (2–4 Weeks)

**Pre-Production Meeting (PPM)**
- 2–4 hour formal meeting: director, production company, agency, client
- Director presents final plan: locations, cast, looks, technical approach, schedule
- All key creative decisions locked or confirmed here
- After PPM, it's very difficult to change course without client/agency approval

**Casting**
- Director involved in casting brief; selects from options by casting director
- Bigger jobs: director attends sessions in person or via tape
- Smaller/direct work: director may handle casting directly

**Location Scouting**
- Director attends tech scout once locations shortlisted
- Evaluates for: creative fit, lighting quality, practical logistics
- May veto locations that don't match treatment aesthetic

**Crew Selection / Department Head Hiring**
- Director consulted on DP choice (or IS the DP)
- Input on: production designer, wardrobe stylist, hair/makeup key
- Strong directors bring preferred DP and production designer relationships to each job

**Storyboarding / Shot Listing**
- Director develops shot list from treatment and PPM
- Larger spots: dedicated storyboard artist hired
- 1st AD builds production schedule from director's shot list

**Technical Prep**
- Camera test day: format, lenses, LUT development, look development
- Look books shared with agency/client for alignment

**Wardrobe / Props / Art Direction Oversight**
- Director reviews and approves hero looks with stylist
- Reviews set dressing and props with art director/production designer
- Talent fittings 1–2 days before shoot

## 6. PRODUCTION WORKFLOW

### The Director's On-Set Role

The director is the creative authority on set. The 1st AD runs the floor (time, crew, schedule). The director delivers the creative vision within that framework.

**Key responsibilities:**
- Approving shots before moving on
- Directing talent performance
- Collaborating with DP on camera, lens, lighting
- Monitoring playback and approving takes
- Maintaining creative consistency across the shoot day
- Managing the video village / agency monitor

### When the Director IS the DP (Joey's Model)

**Advantages:**
- Total creative control of the image
- No translation layer between vision and camera execution
- More efficient for single-camera shoots
- Lower budget shoots prefer the all-in model

**Challenges:**
- Hard to direct talent while operating camera — eye is on frame, not performance
- Risk of being perceived as "cameraman who directs" rather than "director who cameras"

**Practical solutions for larger shoots:**
1. Mount a second monitor so agency/client can watch playback
2. Brief 1st AD to strictly manage time so you can review playback properly
3. Use an experienced AC who can manage camera when you step to talent
4. Brief talent before each setup so in-take adjustments are refinements, not starts
5. Consider 2nd shooter / B-camera for alternate angles

### Agency/Client Video Village Dynamics

- Agency and client watch a dedicated monitor
- Director should come to the village between takes to review playback WITH the team
- Common mistake: directors who never leave the camera create anxiety with agency/client
- Clients give messaging/dialogue notes; agency creatives give framing/performance notes; director decides how to incorporate

### Talent Direction

- **Actors:** Give direction in terms of intention/relationship/emotional reality, not "happy/sad." One note at a time.
- **Real people / non-actors:** Reduce pressure; more takes needed; keep it conversational; use talking points not scripts
- **Product/demo:** Precise framing, hands, product handling
- **Multi-day shoots:** Build rapport early day 1; protect talent energy

## 7. POST-PRODUCTION WORKFLOW

### Director's Cut

- The director's first edit, assembled to their creative vision, before agency/client involvement
- **National TV spots:** delivered 2–5 business days after picture wrap
- **Smaller digital/brand work:** 5–10 business days
- Protected by DGA contract for union work; negotiated in non-union

### Revision Rounds

- **Round 1:** Director's cut to agency; consolidated notes
- **Round 2:** Agency/client cut with notes incorporated; client final notes
- **Round 3:** Final approval cut
- **Total typical revisions:** 2–3 rounds for :30s spot; 3–5 rounds for brand films and corporate

### Director Involvement in Color, Sound, Music

**Color grade:**
- Director present for session or approves remotely
- National spots: 1–2 days at a post house
- Smaller work: colorist works from director's notes; director approves via proxy review

**Sound design and mix:**
- Director reviews and approves final mix
- Input on: SFX selection, ambient sound, music timing, VO pacing
- Mix sessions: 4–8 hours for :30s; 1–2 days for longer work

**Music:**
- Director provides music direction from treatment (reference tracks, style)
- Original music: composer engaged post-picture lock; director collaborates on mood/pacing
- Licensed music: music supervisor handles licensing; director approves selection

### When Directors Hand Off vs. Stay Involved

- **Minimum (execution-only):** Director delivers shoot; editor + agency handle post
- **Standard:** Director delivers cut, approves color, approves final mix
- **Full involvement (brand films, large campaigns):** Director through all post stages including VFX, music supervision, final QC

## 8. HOW DIRECTORS GET HIRED

### The Rep / Agent Model

**Production company affiliation (standard path):**
- Most commercial directors are represented by a production company
- The prod co's EP maintains agency relationships and pitches the director
- Prod co marks up all crew, production costs, and director fees (typically 15–25% markup)
- Director does NOT pay commission directly — prod co makes money on production markup

**Commercial talent agents:**
- Top-tier directors may also have agents (WME, CAA commercial division)
- Agent commissions: typically 10–15% of director's personal fee
- Most mid-tier and regional directors do NOT have separate agents

**Non-exclusive vs. exclusive:**
- Exclusive: one prod co only
- Non-exclusive: work with multiple prod cos
- For emerging directors: non-exclusive is often better to start

### Reel Requirements

- **Length:** 1:30–3:00 primary reel. Shorter is better. Best work first.
- **Must show:** Talent direction and story, not just beautiful imagery
- **A directing reel is NOT a DP reel** — agencies look for performance direction, emotional landing, creative vision

**What kills a reel:**
- Slow pacing
- Inconsistent quality levels
- All one type of work
- No faces / no human performance

### The Direct-to-Brand Path (Joey's Near-Term Play)

1. **Identify DFW brands:** AT&T, American Airlines, Toyota USA, Keurig Dr Pepper, CBRE, Capital One, Match Group, Samsung (Plano)
2. **Target VP of Brand/Marketing or Director of Content**
3. **Lead with your work, not a rate card**
4. **Propose a small first project** — build the relationship
5. **Leverage your credits** — Super Bowl, SpaceX, U.S. Polo Assn. give you instant credibility

## 9. THE TRIPLE-BID SYSTEM EXPLAINED

### What It Is
The standard procurement process for commercial production. Requires the agency to solicit bids from at least three production companies (and their directors) before awarding.

### How It Works (Director's Side)

**Step 1:** Agency develops shortlist of 5–15 directors, narrows to 3

**Step 2:** Creative calls (30 min each); director receives brief, storyboards, references

**Step 3:** Director writes treatment (5–7 days); production company builds AICP budget; both submitted together

**Step 4:** Agency reviews all three bids; selects "recommend" for client approval

**Step 5:** One director awarded; others informed they didn't win (often no detailed feedback)

### Strategy for Emerging Directors

- **Be selective:** Don't bid every job. Bid where you have a genuine creative angle.
- **Budget matters:** Even if your treatment wins creatively, if your budget is high, you can lose.
- **Know when you're the "token" bid:** If a prestigious director is already informally attached and you're the 3rd bid, your odds are very low.
- **The secret to winning:** Treatments that show you've studied the BRAND (not just the script) perform better.

## 10. DEAL TERMS AND BUSINESS PRACTICES

### AICP Framework

- **AICP Bid Form:** Introduced 1975, last major revision 2023. The de facto standard for commercial production estimates.
- **Sections:** A (Pre-Production), B (Shoot Days), C (Post-Production), P (Usage/Markups)
- **Standard markup:** 15–25% production company markup on all line items
- Low-budget threshold: shoot day costs under $150K, total budgets under $700K

### Usage Rights and Director's Fee

Usage categories that affect fee:
- **Internal only:** Lowest value
- **Regional broadcast:** Mid-range
- **National broadcast:** Higher value
- **Global:** Premium
- **Digital/online:** Define platforms (owned vs. paid social vs. all digital)
- **In perpetuity vs. time-limited:** In perpetuity worth more

### Exclusivity Clauses

- National TV: typically 1 year per competitive category
- Corporate/direct: less commonly enforced
- Be careful about overly broad exclusivity that restricts your market

### Kill Fees

- After treatment but before award: No standard fee
- After award, before pre-production: 15–25% of director fee
- After pre-production begins: 25–50%
- After shoot begins: 50–100% plus confirmed expenses
- **Always negotiate kill fees into your contract**

### Overtime and Overages

- DGA: OT at 12 hours; double-time at 18 hours
- Non-union: negotiated; typical is time-and-a-half at 12 hours
- Additional shoot days from scope changes = billed at confirmed day rate

## 12. THE DP-TO-DIRECTOR TRANSITION PATH

### Why DPs Make Natural Commercial Directors
- Deep visual storytelling understanding
- Camera/lighting knowledge = faster problem-solving on set
- DP work involves intense collaboration with directors — you've learned from the inside
- Technical credibility with crew is instant
- Owner/operator model is well-understood in the commercial industry

### The Core Challenge: Perception vs. Reality

The industry will initially read you as a "DP who also directs." The goal is to flip to "director who also DPs."

**Practical steps:**
1. Create a dedicated director's reel immediately — separate from DP reel
2. Start billing as a director first — on rate cards, invoices, website
3. Seek projects where the directing role is primary
4. Build a treatment writing practice now
5. Separate your outreach identity — reach out as a director who owns camera equipment

### The Credits Elevation Path

| Stage | What Joey Has | What to Build Next |
|---|---|---|
| **Now** | 20+ yrs DP; Super Bowl; US Polo global; SpaceX; JFK doc | 3–5 strong directing credits with talent direction; a directing reel |
| **6–18 months** | Directing reel; 3–5 notable director credits | Regional TV/national digital directing credits under "directed by" billing |
| **18–36 months** | Regional and national digital portfolio | 1–2 agency-bought commercials; production company relationship |
| **3–5 years** | Strong directing portfolio; agency recognition | Major brand commercial credits; potential prod company signing |

### Making the Transition Without Starving
- Don't quit DP work immediately — it pays the bills and keeps you on sets
- Use every DP job as directing education: study how directors work with agency/client
- Identify 2–3 direct-to-brand clients who trust you as DP and start the "I also direct" conversation
- Price directing separately from DP from day one — conditions the market

## 13. RED FLAGS AND MISTAKES TO AVOID

### Business/Deal Mistakes

1. **Writing treatments for too many jobs without winning** — Track your treatment-to-win ratio. If you're never winning, something is wrong.

2. **No kill fee clause** — If you prep for 2 weeks and the client cancels, you should be compensated.

3. **Not defining usage rights** — "In perpetuity" for a $3K corporate video that runs nationally for 5 years is a problem.

4. **Being exclusive to a prod co that doesn't bring you work** — Favor non-exclusive to start.

5. **Underpricing to "get in the door"** — Low pricing attracts low-budget clients and conditions the market. Exception: strategic spec work for reel-building.

6. **Confusing DP day rate with director day rate** — Director rate reflects creative vision, leadership, treatment writing, pre/post involvement. Should be higher than comparable DP rate.

7. **No contract for direct-to-brand work** — Always have a letter of agreement covering: scope, deliverables, revisions, payment terms, usage.

### Creative/Reel Mistakes

8. **Same reel for director and DP pitches** — Different audiences, different needs. Separate them.

9. **Reel with no human performance** — Product-only reels don't demonstrate directing ability.

10. **Spec that looks like spec** — If you're going to spec, craft a specific aesthetic, not a bad fake commercial.

### Workflow Mistakes

11. **Not confirming creative decisions in writing** — Verbal approval means nothing without email documentation.

12. **Not visiting the video village** — Ignoring clients on set creates anxiety. Check in regularly.

13. **Handing off post entirely on credited projects** — If the agency cuts something you didn't approve and it airs with your name, you lose control of your portfolio quality.
