# 1. HOW COMMERCIAL JOBS FLOW

## The Pipeline

1. A brand/client briefs their ad agency (script, brief, vision, budget)
2. Agency's creative team develops the concept. An agency producer is assigned
3. Agency producer sends the brief to 3 production companies simultaneously (the "triple bid")
4. Each production company submits a director's treatment + AICP budget estimate
5. Agency reviews all three, recommends one to the client
6. Once awarded, the director selects the DP and key department heads
7. The DP is hired by the production company, not the agency

**As a DP, you are almost never bidding. You are being selected by a director who is bidding.** Your job is to be someone directors want attached to their treatments.

## The Model Is Shifting

From Wandering DP Ep. #222 (Kate Downie, EP): The traditional agency-production company-crew hierarchy is becoming more fluid. Brands are sometimes contracting directly with production companies, cutting out agencies. Understanding where a job is coming from matters because it changes the stakeholder dynamics entirely.

## The Availability and Hold System

- **Avail check**: Non-binding inquiry about your availability for specific dates. You can still accept other work
- **First hold / First refusal**: You agree to contact them before accepting a conflicting booking. Still not confirmed
- **On hold / Booked**: Dates locked. If the production cancels, a kill fee is owed
- **Response protocol**: Same-day response to availability requests. Slow responses signal disinterest

# 2. WORKING WITH AGENCIES AND CLIENTS

## Service Work vs. Creative Work (Episode #477)

**The single most important concept for agency relationships.** Not all jobs demand the same level of creative input:

- **Service work**: The client/agency has a fully formed vision. They need skilled execution, not creative input
- **Creative-led work**: They want your creative voice, your aesthetic, your ideas

**Misreading which type of job you're on creates the most friction.** Pushing creative agenda on service work creates problems. Being passive on creative-led work is a missed opportunity.

## How to Read Which Mode You're In

- Did they approach you because of your reel and aesthetic? Creative partner mode is possible
- Did they approach you because you're available and affordable? Service mode
- Did the director personally request you? They want your voice
- Did a producer call and mention rates before the brief? Service mode

From Ep. #86: "These jobs are textbook retail commercial cinematography. You have a skill that can help someone make something people want to buy. It may not align with your own sensibilities but you can still work hard to make it look rich and interesting."

## The Four Stakeholders

On any agency job, the DP navigates four parties simultaneously:

1. **The Director** — primary creative collaborator, on-set authority
2. **The Producer** — controls schedule, budget, logistics
3. **The Agency** — owns the creative brief, answers to the client
4. **The Client** — the brand, the one paying for everything

Each has different agendas. The DP must navigate all four without alienating any.

## Handling Agency Direction on Set

**The chain of command is clear:** The DP takes direction from the director. Period.

When the agency tries to give the DP direct direction, redirect: "That's a really useful note. Let me loop in [director's name] so we can figure out the best way to address it." Do not implement changes that contradict the director's plan without the director's knowledge.

When the director and agency are in conflict on set, stay neutral, keep shooting, and let the two of them work it out through the producer.

## When to Speak Up and When to Take a Loss

Speaking up is appropriate:
- In pre-production, when you identify a technical problem with the plan
- When something is physically impossible in the allotted time
- When you have specific expertise that changes the outcome

Taking a loss means:
- The agency creative director wants a different look than you planned
- The client wants to change the color palette
- Someone senior in the chain has made a decision and you disagree aesthetically

**Voice your professional concern clearly, once, through the right channel. Then execute.**

> "I want to flag something. If we do it this way, we risk [specific problem]. One alternative would be [X]. What would you like to do?"

That's it. One clear statement. Then execute the answer.

## Red Flags in Initial Contact (Episode #100)

When initial contact comes in, gather five things before responding with rates:

1. **Who is asking**: Production company? Agency producer? Director? Each signals a different type of job
2. **The brief/script**: Ask for it immediately. No brief, no quote
3. **Timeline**: Shoot dates and whether prep time is real
4. **Deliverables**: How many spots? What formats? Broadcast or digital?
5. **Budget range**: Ask what they've allocated for your position

Red flags:
- No creative brief or treatment available
- "We'll figure out the look on the day"
- Rate requests with no project details attached
- Very fast turnaround with no mention of prep time
- Requests to quote before seeing the script
- Agency or client contacting the DP directly rather than going through the production company

## Agency Control Over Content

As O'Sullivan's commercial work scaled, agencies became reluctant to let him publicly post commercial breakdowns. Some breakdown episodes moved to Patreon. **This is itself a data point on how agencies think about content control.** Directly relevant to portfolio/media clauses in NDAs and contracts.

# 3. WORKING WITH DIRECTORS

## First Meeting with a New Director

**Come prepared, not passive.** From Sony Cine: "It's not enough for a DP just to listen at a first interview; they have to contribute ideas if they want to get the job." Bring 3-4 different creative approaches, a dozen reference images, or physical art books.

> "I am always happier to meet a potential department head who felt inspired enough by the material that they arrive at the interview bubbling with ideas. It is easier to redirect energy than to generate it."

**Read the treatment before the first phone call.** The treatment is what got the director the job. Break it down by scene using the 5 Ws (who, what, when, where, why) before you speak.

**Listen first, then mirror.** From the Wandering DP gear article: "I kept listening for clues from the director and the more and more he described the project the more I started to form my vision."

**Discuss vision, not virtues.** Directors already have agents pitching people. Focus on creative ideas for this specific project, not a list of your accomplishments.

**Interview them too.** From a British Cinematographer feature: "I want to come into that meeting with a point of view because immediately you're going to know whether a director is a collaborator or they have their own mindset. I'm interviewing the director as much as he is interviewing me."

## What Directors Actually Evaluate

From Sony Cine's multi-part series:
- Calmness, maturity, and organization
- Whether the DP will be a good leader of their department
- Whether they would enjoy being next to this person 12-14 hours a day
- How they manage their team and whether they command loyalty
- Technical skill is an assumed baseline, not a differentiator

## What Directors Actually Want from Their DP

- **Creative partnership, not technical execution.** "All directors are usually working at the limits of their physical and mental capacity. They want the DP to step up and make suggestions, then run with that vision and become full creative partners."
- **Showing is better than describing.** Bring visual materials, not verbal pitches
- **Clear communication and a private channel for problems.** Never publicly contradict the director
- **Speed without sacrificing quality.** "Slow cinematographers aren't desired collaborators." Speed is a creative virtue, not a compromise
- **A proven crew you bring with you.** Directors are hiring the camera and lighting department you assemble, not just you
- **Serve the director's vision, not your own.** "You are there to serve the director. Thorough preparation lets you anticipate director needs and reduce production stress."

## Handling Creative Disagreements on Set

- **Do not react in the moment.** Directors often regret anger-fueled remarks due to pressure
- **Never ridicule the director to crew members.** This is irreversible damage to the production
- **Private, specific, non-personal language.** "Discussions must address specific incidents, never ad hominem attacks. Avoid words like 'always' and 'never.'"
- **Never claim technical impossibility dishonestly.** Claiming shots will take longer than they will to avoid doing them is "an old, sad trick" that destroys trust
- **When time runs out, your preferences yield to the director's.** "The last thing that should be sacrificed are those elements the director wants."
- **The reputational calculus:** "No one will remember the awful circumstances that led to the DP's bad behaviour; they will only remember the bad behaviour."

## Building Repeat Relationships

- **Exceed expectations on execution.** Not just on the reel, but on how the day runs
- **Be a "production favorite."** Other department heads enjoy the working environment you create
- **Keep your crew happy.** Your crew's morale is visible to the director
- **Stay demonstrably loyal.** "DPs must actively demonstrate support. They have to let the director know they are loyal. That is part of the job."
- **Give credit; do not self-aggrandize.** Directors notice who makes them look good
- **Kenneth Branagh's three rules:** Be on time. Be polite. Know your job. "Everything else is luck."

## On-Set Dynamics with the Director

- **Position yourself physically near the director and monitor.** The director-DP conversation happens in real time as you watch takes together
- **React visibly and honestly to takes.** The director uses you as a first-eye validation tool
- **Keep quiet once actors arrive.** "A quiet set makes for better performances."
- **Share vision with the crew.** Brief your gaffer and key grip on the visual direction, not just technical specs
- **Watch dailies together.** "Taking the time to watch dailies together really builds a bond and gets them completely aligned."
- **Resist producer side-deals that undermine the director.** If a producer asks you to advocate against the director, decline

# 4. WORKING WITH PRODUCERS

## How Producers Evaluate DPs

- They watch reels on mute first to assess actual cinematography
- They want complete scenes and structured coverage, not disconnected beautiful frames
- When narrowed to 3-4 candidates, the reel no longer matters. The conversation does
- They call references from directors and crews who've worked with you
- They ask specifically how you handled difficult situations
- Reputation within the regional production community matters. Commercial production is a small world

## Budget Conversations

**The first call:** Line producers want to communicate budget constraints early. If they don't volunteer the budget range, ask directly: "What's the budget allocated for the DP role?"

**Never open with your number.** Ask what they've budgeted. This anchors the conversation to their ceiling, not a lowball.

**If their offer is below your rate:** Ask what else is negotiable (equipment rental credit, prep days, additional shoot days).

**Understand the markup reality:** Your $1,500/day rate appears in the AICP bid as $2,000+ once P&W fringes (24-34%) are applied, plus the production company's overhead markup (15-25%). This explains why productions push back hard on DP rates even on large-budget jobs.

## Schedule Negotiation

- **Prep days** appear in AICP Section A, budgeted separately from shoot days
- For commercials: typically 1-5 prep days depending on complexity
- Prep days should cover: location scout, tech scout with gaffer, camera package review, director meetings
- **Every extra prep day comes from somewhere in the budget.** "You want to make sure that you're not asking for the moon and getting it, only to find out later they took a day off your shooting schedule to pay for it."
- Overtime after camera wrap requires producer approval before it's worked

## Managing the Producer Relationship on Set

- The 1st AD manages schedule execution on set, not the producer directly
- The AD/DP relationship is "the most important one on set"
- Before the shoot, sit down with the AD and prioritize the shot list: priority shots vs. bonus shots
- Frame creative needs in production language: "I need 20 minutes to get this right, and if I don't have it, we won't have anything usable"
- Schedule awareness is not optional. The DP who manages "not only the look of the shot but the time it takes" earns producer trust

## Handling Scope Creep

When a producer or agency rep asks for something outside scope mid-production:

1. Acknowledge it: "That's a great idea, let's make sure we can capture that"
2. Name the impact: "To do this properly, we'll need [X time / Y resource]. Do you want me to flag this for the line producer?"
3. Get it in writing: A text or email confirmation is sufficient

**Never say "that's not in the scope" as a closing statement.** Say "that's not in the current scope. Let's figure out how to make it happen."

## Deliverables the Producer Needs

During production:
- Camera reports (daily, typically generated by 2nd AC but DP is responsible for accuracy)
- LUT package and monitoring setup
- Media organization per agreed convention, backups confirmed
- Dailies within agreed window (often same-day or next-morning)

Post-production:
- Availability for a color grading session (day rate applies)
- Technical delivery specs confirmed in writing before production begins

# 5. BUILDING AND MANAGING CREW

## The DP's Chain of Communication

The DP communicates directly with department heads. Those heads relay to their crews. You do not give instructions to the best boy, the electrics, or the grips directly.

### With the Gaffer

- Send lighting plans and references before the tech scout
- At the tech scout, walk every setup and talk through each one
- On set, stay close enough to whisper adjustments. The DP whispers to the gaffer, who radios to the crew
- Pull the gaffer to the monitor so they see what you see
- From Ep. #478: Do not ask the gaffer to throw a light into the ceiling to boost ambient as a first instinct. It scatters light everywhere and is impossible to control

**Once you work with the same gaffer repeatedly**, they learn your preferred fixtures. Communication compresses dramatically. New gaffers require more explicit communication.

### With the Key Grip

- Key grip and gaffer are peers, both reporting to you
- Communicate camera support needs: dolly, crane, Steadicam, gimbal, car mount
- Negative fill positions, diffusion frames, bounce cards
- Run unconventional rigging ideas by the key grip before committing to the schedule
- On union sets, the boundary is absolute: grips handle non-electrical rigging, electricians handle anything with a power cable

### With the 1st AC

Before camera prep, give them:
- Camera body, lenses, and accessories list confirmed
- Number of cameras
- Camera support needs
- Shooting specs: resolution, codec, frame rate, log profile
- Baseline exposure settings

On set: when you want a lens swap or camera adjustment, say it once to the 1st AC. They run the camera department. They tell you when the camera is ready.

### With the DIT

Before the first day:
- The visual look and mood for the project
- Exposure preferences
- LUT requirements
- Color space and gamma curve for post workflow
- Whether you want CDLs saved shot-by-shot

The DIT is your best and often only chance to impose your vision on the final color in commercial work. Over-communicate on first-time collaborations.

## Finding and Vetting Crew

**Primary methods (in order of reliability):**
1. Personal referrals from other DPs
2. Ask your department heads to recommend their lateral peers
3. Your rental house (camera houses know ACs, lighting houses know gaffers)
4. Industry directories (ProductionHUB, Mandy)
5. Social media production groups for your city

**What to look for in a strong gaffer:**
- Anticipates what you need before you ask
- Offers technical suggestions based on expertise
- Executes changes quickly and cleanly
- Works with minimal lights when appropriate
- Gets it the first time

**What to look for in a strong 1st AC:**
- Strong focus pulling in your typical conditions
- Takes ownership of the camera package
- Communicates clearly about what is and isn't working
- Gets along with the 2nd AC

**Integrating new crew members:** When working with locals you haven't used before, over-communicate in pre-production. Send more documentation, not less. Be explicit about preferences you'd normally leave implied.

## Handling Crew Performance Issues

Step 1: **Address it through the department head.** "Hey, I need X to happen faster. Can you work with your team on that?"

Step 2: **Be specific about the problem, not the person.** "The turnaround is running 20 minutes longer than we can afford" works. "Your best boy is incompetent" creates defensiveness.

Step 3: **For direct reports (1st AC, operator):** Address it in private. State the specific behavior and impact. Give them the chance to correct it.

Step 4: **Escalation.** If affecting production and the department head can't fix it, escalate to the producer. Frame it as a production problem: "We are losing time on setup because X is not functioning at the level we need. I need help solving this."

From Ep. #489 ("Arranging Deck Chairs"): Find small, repeatable improvements without becoming disruptive. Diplomatic implementation over confrontation.

## Maintaining Crew Relationships

- **Bring people back.** Book them first on the next job before looking elsewhere
- **Communicate after the job.** A short message saying the footage looked great costs nothing and builds loyalty
- **Refer them to other DPs.** The goodwill compounds
- **Respect their expertise.** Do not micromanage
- **Pay on time.** Nothing damages a relationship faster than late payment
- **Credit your department heads to production.** When the director compliments a setup, say "that was the gaffer's solution." Crew members remember this

# 6. ON-SET POLITICS AND PROFESSIONALISM

## The DP's Three Roles (Episode #503, Jared Levy)

### 1. Image-Making
The craft: exposure, lighting, composition, camera movement.

### 2. Managerial
Running the department, keeping the day moving, managing crew dynamics. "Being able to hustle your crew together and keep the day moving on set isn't going above and beyond your job description. It is your job description."

### 3. Political
The relational, interpersonal dimension. The least-discussed pillar and often the one that makes or breaks a career.

> "DPs who are social or political animals often rise to the top because people want to work with them." Agents explicitly say they have to "sell your disposition as well as your talent."

**Audit yourself on all three pillars.** Your political capital is built in pre-production, between takes, at wrap dinners, and in how you talk about past jobs.

## Taste vs. Judgement (Episode #476)

**Taste** is knowing what good looks like. **Judgement** is knowing what will move the needle on this specific production. A DP can have excellent taste but poor judgement if they can't calibrate choices to the actual constraints and goals of a given project.

## The Political DP

1. **Builds relationships before the shoot.** Tech scouts and PPMs are trust-building exercises
2. **Manages up through the director.** Quietly flags building agency anxiety: "The agency seems nervous about the low-key lighting. Might be worth a quick word."
3. **Keeps the department invisible and efficient.** Every visible argument in the camera department costs the DP politically
4. **Does not gossip about other jobs.** What you say about a difficult director will reach that director. The industry is structurally small

## Video Village Politics

- **Center positions**: Director, DP
- **Adjacent**: Script supervisor, 1st AD
- **Behind**: Agency producer, agency creative
- **Further back**: Client (brand representative)

A tighter, less accessible setup physically limits the number of people who crowd in and offer unsolicited notes.

**Handling unsolicited notes from video village:** Receive it graciously ("Thanks, I'll flag that"), relay it to the appropriate person (director or producer), keep moving. Do not argue, do not ignore entirely. Receive and redirect.

## Conflicting Direction

When the agency art director comes to video village and tells you to change the color temperature:

> "I hear you. The current setup is [X]. Let me get [director] over here so we can make that call together."

Bring the director over. They work it out with the agency. You execute the result.

**Pre-production is the real battleground.** Whatever is agreed in the PPM is your operating authority on set. If an on-set request conflicts with PPM decisions, you have a documented baseline to refer back to.

## Handling Difficult Personalities

- **Never quit mid-production.** "If you have to leave, get fired." Quitting is a permanent reputation mark
- **You cannot change them; you can only change your reaction.** Work around the behavior, not the person
- **Stay calm.** The crew looks to the DP for emotional tone. If you're calm, the department is calm
- **Address behavior, not character.** "I'd appreciate if we could discuss technical questions before we roll" is specific. "You're being disrespectful" opens a fight
- **Use the producer as a buffer.** Your line of escalation is the line producer, not direct confrontation
- **Document privately.** If an interaction crosses a professional or safety line, note the time, what was said, and who was present

## Being Asked to Do Something Technically Wrong

Step 1: Identify it precisely. "If we shoot with that lens at that aperture in this light, we will not have a usable image."

Step 2: State it once, clearly, to the director. "I need to flag a technical concern before we proceed."

Step 3: Offer an alternative. "We could achieve the same result by doing X instead."

Step 4: If they proceed over your objection, execute it and document: "Just for the record, I want to confirm you're aware this may not be technically achievable. I'll do my best."

**Technical errors (wrong exposure, continuity, safety): speak up every time.** Creative disagreements: voice it once, then execute their version with full commitment.

## When to Walk Away

**Before the job — red flags:**
- Vague or nonexistent deal memo past deadline
- Reluctance to put terms in writing
- Pressure to start prep before contracts are signed
- Budget incompatible with creative demands
- No interest in a pre-production conversation

**During production:**
- Safety violations are the clearest case
- Raise your concern to the line producer in writing
- State specifically what needs to change
- Give them opportunity to address it
- Never walk in the middle of a take or without notice

# 7. SET ETIQUETTE — THE UNWRITTEN RULES

## Foundational Rules

- **"On time" means early.** If you arrive at call time, you're late
- **Silence when rolling.** No side conversations. No walkie chatter. Not negotiable
- **Never touch what is not in your department.** Not your department, not your equipment, not your decision
- **"Crossing."** Call it out when walking in front of the lens. Walk behind the camera whenever possible
- **No food or drink near camera equipment.** Nothing goes on the camera cart or cases
- **Chain of command is not optional.** Ideas surface through the hierarchy. They don't skip levels
- **Your opinion on other departments is not wanted.** Your domain is the image. Stay in it
- **Never disappear.** Tell someone in your department where you're going
- **Introduce yourself to everyone on day one.** People go out of their way for people they have a name and a face for
- **The phone.** Silent. In pocket

## DP-Specific Etiquette

- **Do not show the director a shot before it's ready.** When you say "ready," it means ready
- **The monitor is for the director, not a committee.** Be polite but clear about creative authority
- **Protect the gaffer's authority.** Go to the gaffer privately about crew issues, never around them
- **Credit your department heads publicly.** This is how long-term loyalty gets built
- **Introduce unfamiliar techniques diplomatically.** "I want to try something. Let's see if this works" beats a directive from the top

## Walkie-Talkie Protocol

Say the person's name first ("Gaffer, DP"), wait for acknowledgment, then deliver the message. Do not transmit and assume it was received.

## The DP Sets the Emotional Tone

> "The cinematographer's personality spreads throughout the set, affecting everyone from actors to crew."

A difficult DP creates a set culture that slows down production, damages creativity, and ensures they don't get called back. A calm, organized DP creates an environment where crew does their best work.

# 8. RATES, BIDS, AND MONEY

## Rate Negotiation

**Hold your rate when:**
- The production has a legitimate budget and is trying their luck
- You're in demand with other options
- The rate is below your established minimum
- The job is unattractive for other reasons

**Flex your rate when:**
- The relationship matters more than the day rate (new director you want to build with)
- The project will produce strong reel material
- The production is genuinely budget-constrained and you believe in it
- You're trying to break into a new category

**Specific tactics:**
- Attach gear rental to supplement your rate without arguing the day rate directly (Ep. #462)
- If you have another offer at a higher rate, say so directly: "I have another offer for more, I really want to do your project, what can you do?"
- Ask the "lever question": "What have you allocated for the DP?" Not "what's your budget"

| Camera Operator | $550 - $650 |

**IATSE Union minimums (LA, 2025-2026, 8-hour day):** DP $1,234/day, Operator $756, 1st AC $547, DIT $720.

**Overtime:** Standard commercial day is 10 hours. Hours 10-12: time-and-a-half. After 12: double-time.

## The AICP Bid Structure

The AICP form organizes costs into lettered sections:
- **A-B:** Pre-production/wrap labor and shooting crew (DP day rate lives in Section B)
- **C-D:** Pre-production expenses and location/travel
- **E-H:** Makeup/wardrobe, studio, art department
- **I:** Equipment costs (camera, grip, electric packages)
- **J-K:** Media and miscellaneous
- **L:** Director/creative fees
- **M-N:** Talent labor and expenses
- **O-P:** Other expenses and post-production

**Markups on every line:**
- Production fees: 15-25% of total budget (production company overhead and profit)
- P&W fringes: 24-34% on top of every crew day rate
- Insurance: ~2.5% of total budget
- Payroll taxes and handling fees

**Three budget versions maintained:** Awarded Estimate (approved original), Working (real-time committed), Actual (spent from POs and payroll).

**Payment terms (AICP standard):** 50% within 5 business days of award, 75% before delivery.

## Gear Ownership as Business Strategy (Episode #462)

O'Sullivan pushes back on the misconception that owning gear isn't worth it. Owning and renting gear to productions "has definitely warranted the additional work." Run the numbers yourself.

Attaching your own gear rental to a production is a legitimate way to raise your effective rate without arguing the day rate.

# 9. CONTRACTS, INSURANCE, AND PROTECTION

## Deal Memo Essentials

Address every problem before doing any work, in writing, signed by both parties:
- Day rate and overtime calculation method
- Kit rental amount
- Kill fee (10-25% is standard)
- Travel, per diem, and meal provisions
- Payment timeline and method
- Prep and wrap day rates
- Reel/portfolio usage rights

## Insurance

In standard commercial production:
- **Production company** carries: General Liability ($1M minimum), Equipment Insurance, Workers' Comp, Commercial Auto
- If you own camera equipment and rent it to the production, get explicit written confirmation their equipment policy covers your gear
- Total insurance cost: typically ~2.5% of production budget

## Documentation Practices

- **Email trails are your record.** After a creative decision is made verbally on set, send a brief email that evening: "Following up on today. Wanted to confirm we're going with [X] for tomorrow's setup as discussed."
- **Maintain a private "show bible."** A log of each production, key players, how decisions were made, what red flags appeared

## On Replacing Another DP

If you're offered a job and discover the previous DP was fired, contact the replaced DP directly before accepting. Professional solidarity and your own intelligence-gathering about why they were let go is worth the five-minute call.

# 10. PORTFOLIO AND REEL MANAGEMENT

## What Agents and Producers Evaluate

- They stop watching if not engaged in the first 10-30 seconds
- Three exceptional pieces beat twelve average ones
- Does it show a consistent point of view, not just technical competence?
- "We've all seen reels with lens flares and open fields. That's exhausting." (Kristen Tolle-Billings, WPA)
- Lead with your absolute best
- Sequences are more convincing than isolated shots
- No outdated work, no standard definition

## Usage Rights and Embargoes

- Commercial work legally belongs to the client. You don't have automatic portfolio rights
- Build a portfolio clause into your deal memo: "I reserve the right to use this work in my professional portfolio"
- Never use commercial footage publicly without permission
- Embargoes are real. Work may need to stay off your reel until the campaign goes public
- Ask the producer at wrap: "When the campaign is live, is there any issue with me including clips on my reel?" Getting it on record protects you

## Beyond the Reel

- Website showing personality and aesthetic, not just credits
- Instagram presence matters increasingly for commercial DPs
- **Do the work you want to attract before you have permission.** If you want to shoot fashion campaigns, shoot fashion on your own

## Getting Agent Representation (Episodes #87, #374)

- Don't seek an agent until you need one. It "sorts itself out when it's supposed to"
- Agents find you more than you find them. Show your work at every opportunity
- The agent gets you in the room; getting the job is on you
- Personal recommendations are the strongest path
- Agents want longevity clients who can work across categories
- Agents take 10-15% commission
- They negotiate rates, field availability inquiries, pitch you to producers and directors, and guide which jobs to take

# 11. PRE-PRODUCTION AS A BUSINESS PRACTICE

## The Visual Lookbook

From the Wandering DP: "You can talk your way through a setup or idea for an hour, or you can show a basic reference and have everyone on the same page in under a minute."

- **A necessity when working with new directors**, a luxury with established collaborators
- Three categories: location lighting references, mood lighting references, camera angles and lens selection
- Cull aggressively with a 1-5 rating system. Allocate specific images to specific scenes
- **Annotate every image** with what specific quality it demonstrates
- **Present it professionally.** Polished InDesign layouts, not a folder of JPEGs dropped in email
- **Print it for scouts.** The lookbook travels with you

## The Tech Scout

O'Sullivan calls this "quite possibly the most important time for the DP in pre-production."

**Bring:**
- Camera with a zoom lens to show actual focal lengths
- Printed lighting diagrams with location-specific notes
- A light meter to assess ambient light
- Final working storyboards

**The purpose:** Leave every location with all department heads aligned on the plan. Not to improvise on the day.

## Pre-Production Communication Timeline

**2-4 weeks out:**
- Send gaffer location scout images, initial lighting references, visual direction notes
- Confirm camera package with 1st AC
- Book DIT and have initial call about post workflow

**1-2 weeks out:**
- Send gaffer updated lighting diagrams with fixture placement, power needs, rigging points
- Give 1st AC shooting specs (codec, resolution, frame rate, log profile)
- Confirm camera support needs with 1st AC and key grip
- Give DIT finalized LUT direction

**Tech scout (1-3 days before):**
- Walk every setup with gaffer
- Measure ambient light levels
- Test fixture placement
- Confirm rigging and power access
- Flag problems while they can still be solved

**Night before shoot:**
- Shot list with setups in shooting order reaches 1st AC and gaffer

## The BODVO Framework

O'Sullivan's system for structuring every initial client conversation:
- **B**udget
- **O**bjectives
- **D**eliverables
- **V**ision
- **O**bstacles

## The Single Governing Question

> **"What are we going for?"**

Every technical decision flows from this. Camera, lens, lighting package, aesthetic choices. Gear decisions made before this question is answered are backwards.

# 13. CRAFT FUNDAMENTALS — QUICK REFERENCE

## Lighting

- **Ratios, not raw light quantity.** Key:Fill and Key:Background are the two measurements that matter
- **When you can't control a source** (sun, practicals), use it as the ratio determinant and build around it
- **Salt and Pepper:** Intentionally alternating light and dark areas within the frame for depth
- **Blocking can solve lighting problems.** Subject placement interacts with available and designed light

## Lens Selection

- Every gear decision answers: "What are we going for?"
- O'Sullivan's preferred spherical focal length: 32mm
- Vintage anamorphic on commercial timelines has practical difficulties. The skill is making those challenges work aesthetically

## Camera Movement

Each movement tool has a specific emotional register:
- **Dolly** — controlled, intentional
- **Steadicam** — smooth with human energy
- **Gimbal** — mechanical precision
- **Handheld** — instability, uncertainty (deliberate choice, not budget workaround)

## Color and LUT Workflow

- **Squeeze and Drop (S+D):** Squeeze the log image (reduce contrast, preserve shadow detail), then drop the levels in the grade. Dark, moody aesthetic with retained shadow detail
- **Two-LUT workflow:** Standard LogC-to-Rec709 on set, S+D for dailies/grade
- **The colorist relationship is a creative partnership** established in pre-production, not fixed in post

# 15. KEY PRINCIPLES SUMMARY

1. **Every decision flows from one question: "What are we going for?"** Answer this before touching any gear
2. **Service work vs. creative work.** Misreading which type of job you're on creates the most friction with agencies and clients
3. **The DP has three roles: image-making, managerial, and political.** The political role is least discussed but often most important
4. **Pre-production determines quality more than on-set execution.** The tech scout is the most important moment
5. **Voice your concern once, through the right channel, then execute.** One clear statement. Then deliver
6. **Character over talent.** Directors hire people they can live with for 14-hour days
7. **Chain of command exists for a reason.** Redirect direction from the wrong person to the right person
8. **The lookbook gets everyone aligned in under a minute.** A description takes an hour
9. **Credit your department heads.** Crew loyalty is built by public recognition
10. **A good reel is great but a good relationship is better.** Repeat work from directors is the actual engine

# DIRECT RELEVANCE TO GLM

## For Cardboard Spaceship and Agency Work

- **Read the room on service vs. creative work.** When an agency brings you onto a job, determine immediately whether they want your creative voice or your execution skills. Both are valuable. Pushing creative when they want execution creates friction
- **Understand the four-stakeholder dynamic.** On any agency job you'll navigate director, producer, agency, and client. Each has different goals
- **Portfolio/media control is standard at the agency level.** The NDA portfolio clause is exactly this dynamic
- **The tech scout is where you prove your value.** This is the moment where agencies and production companies evaluate whether you're a "hire again" DP
- **The PPM is your operating authority.** Whatever is agreed in the pre-production meeting is your documented baseline on set

## For Building GLM's Production Company Side

- **The "Disappearing Middle" is real.** Position above it. The portfolio (SpaceX, ESPN, Ford, Coca-Cola) already signals premium. Don't compete on price
- **Gear ownership is a profit lever.** Charge market rental rate for gear you own
- **Systems thinking scales.** BODVO, The Framework, the Playbook Approach. Build your own systems for repeatable quality
- **Speed is a creative virtue.** Being fast and good is the combination that gets you hired back
