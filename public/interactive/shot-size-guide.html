The Only Shot Size Guide You'll Ever Need

9 essential shots every filmmaker must master. What they do, when to use them, and the film scenes that prove it.

Films are made of sequences. Sequences are made of scenes. Scenes are made of shots. Every shot choice shapes rhythm, tone, and meaning.

Shot selection should be the highest priority for both a director and a DP. The wrong shot at the wrong moment kills a scene. The right one makes it unforgettable.

Here are the 9 shot sizes that make up every film ever made, from Blade Runner to Coco. Each one is a tool with a specific purpose. Master all nine and you'll never be stuck on set wondering what to shoot.

Shot 01
Establishing Shot
The World Builder
Wide enough to establish geography, time of day, and the scale of subjects in their environment. The most common way to open a scene or an entire film.
Scene opener Location transition World-building Time of day
🎬 Blade Runner 2049 opens with establishing shots of industrial farms. First impression of near-future Earth.
Shot 02
Master Shot
The Safety Net
Captures the entire scene playing out. Confirms who's in it, where they are, and their spatial relationships. The editor's fallback.
Full scene coverage Group dynamics Editor safety
🎬 The Godfather Part II — Family dinner in the master emphasizes closeness. Then Michael sits alone and the emptiness hits.
Shot 03
Wide Shot
The Scale Statement
Subject far from camera. Not about location (that's establishing), it's about scale. Makes subjects feel small, lost, overwhelmed, or isolated.
Isolation Loneliness Environment scale
🎬 Phantom Thread — Alma and Reynolds dwarfed by a messy ballroom. Together, yet isolated from the world.

The establishing shot says "here's where we are." The wide shot says "here's how small we are inside it."

Shot 04
Full Shot
Head to Toe
Subject fills the frame from top to bottom. Tight enough for facial storytelling but wide enough for full body, posture, and wardrobe.
Physicality Wardrobe Character entrance
Shot 05
Medium Full / Cowboy
The Power Stance
Head to just below the waist. Named for gun holster height. Reads as confident, dangerous, confrontational. The standoff framing.
Confrontation Power Weapons drawn
🎬 The Favourite — Lady Sarah in cowboy framing with a firearm. Not a western, still unmistakably a standoff.
Shot 06
Medium Shot
The Workhorse
The most popular shot in cinema. Waist to above the head. Similar to how we see people in conversation. Neutral: neither dramatic nor distancing.
Dialogue Neutral ground Eyes + body
🎬 Coco — Miguel's medium shot captures his shrine, the TV, and his reaction all in one intimate frame.

The medium shot is cinema's default because it mirrors how humans actually see each other. It's the handshake distance.

Shot 07
Medium Close-Up
The Focus Pull
Chest to above the head. Strips away distraction. Close enough for emotion, wide enough to include hands, props, or actions at chest level.
Reduce distraction Character + prop Reaction
🎬 Avengers: Endgame — Thanos's snap. MCU frames both the gauntlet and his face. When he fails, we see it all.
Shot 08
Close-Up
The Empathy Engine
The most powerful visual weapon for emotion and dramatic beats. Typically at eye level. Front row seat for thoughts and feelings. This is about empathy.
Emotional turn Decision moment Anxiety Empathy
🎬 The Shining — "Here's Johnny." The close-up as cinema's most devastating weapon.
Shot 09
Extreme Close-Up
The Scalpel
Isolates a specific area: eyes, lips, a prop. The most intimate, dramatic, and potentially startling shot size. Maximum emphasis. Use deliberately.
Maximum emphasis Crucial detail Startling reveal
🎬 Kill Bill Vol. 1 — The Bride's frantic eyes as the Crazy 88 swarm. Pure adrenaline through the eyes alone.

Three Zones
Every Shot Has a Job
🌍
Context
ES · Master · WS
Where are we? Who's here? What's the scale? The orientation shots.
🤝
Engagement
FS · MFS · MS
Conversational range. Body language, posture, wardrobe. Where most runtime lives.
💔
Intimacy
MCU · CU · ECU
Emotion, revelation, emphasis. The audience enters the character's inner world.
