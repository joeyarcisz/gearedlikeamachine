The One-Light Interview Setup

Four decisions. Maximum result.

The best interview lighting you will ever produce comes from one source. Not because you lack a second light. Because you chose not to use one.

That choice is the discipline. Adjust the four controls below and watch how each decision reshapes the subject. Key quality. Negative fill. Background distance. Lighting pattern.

01 — Key Quality DECISION 1

Source size relative to the subject determines shadow edge. Larger apparent source, softer transition. Smaller, harder falloff.

Medium diffusion — a 2x3 Chimera on an M18 or a Skypanel with opal frost. The workhorse for most interview work.
02 — Lighting Pattern DECISION 2

Axis angle determines which pattern falls on the face. Each pattern communicates a different relationship between subject and viewer.

Loop lighting — the DP default. Nose shadow falls toward the corner of the mouth but does not connect with the cheek shadow. Flattering and dimensional without being theatrical.
03 — Negative Fill DECISION 3

Distance of the black V-flat from the shadow side of the subject. Closer absorbs more ambient. Further, shadows lift as room bounce reaches the face.

V-Flat Distance 2.5 ft
On-face (deep shadow) Removed (shadows lift)
2.5 ft — optimal position. Shadow side holds without crushing. The falloff from key to shadow reads as three-dimensional.
04 — Background Distance DECISION 4

Inverse square law does the separation work. Pull the subject away from the background and the key falls off before it reaches the wall.

Subject-to-BG Distance 6 ft
Against wall (0 ft) Max distance (15 ft)
6 ft — minimum workable separation. Key metered at f/4 on the subject falls to roughly f/1.4 at the background. 2.5 stops of natural separation without a second light.

The Catch Light Test

Every setup is complete or incomplete based on one thing. One white shape, upper portion of the eye. If it is missing, the key is too far off-axis. If there are two, a fill crept in. If it is in the lower half, the key is below eye level.

Correct — upper quarter, single source
Two catch lights — fill lamp in setup
Lower catch light — key too low
No catch light — key too far off-axis
"You do not create contrast by adding more light to the bright side. You create contrast by subtracting light from the dark side."

The References

The Fog of War
Errol Morris, 2003
One motivated source per subject. Shadow side holds throughout. The weight in McNamara's face is a lighting decision made before the camera rolled.
Blade Runner 2049
Roger Deakins, 2017
Negative fill baked into the approach. Every shadow side in K's close-ups holds because ambient light was controlled before the key was placed.
The Godfather Part II
Gordon Willis, 1974
Practical fixtures as motivating sources, dimmed below realistic level. Key supplemented off-camera at matching angle. The lamp sells the logic.
Atonement
Seamus McGarvey, 2007
Window motivation on every interior. Large frames outside shape the source quality. Emotional temperature established by light direction before dialogue begins.

When you can produce a compelling, dimensional interview setup from one light consistently, you understand light. Not as a fixture choice. As a system.

The DP who walks in with one Skypanel and a V-flat and produces a better image than the crew that just unloaded six fixtures is not lucky. They know something that six-light crew does not.
