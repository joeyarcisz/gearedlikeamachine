At $2,500/day for 3 days, you're spending $7,500 on labor. Add a $1,200/day camera package and the real number is $11,100. A project rate at 10% off saves you $1,110 and aligns the DP's incentive to your outcome, not the clock.
03 / The Interview

Four Questions That Reveal Everything

Skip the theater. These four questions separate working DPs from talented shooters. Click each to see what strong and weak answers sound like.

Q1 "Walk me through your prep process for a project like this." ▼
Strong Answer

Describes tech scout, lighting design session, conversation with director about focal length choices, camera package decision, brief to the gaffer. Process is defined before call time.

Weak Answer

"I like to show up early and stay flexible." Translation: they have no pre-production process. You will pay for their improvisation in shoot day time.

Q2 "Tell me about a shot you planned that didn't work on the day, and what you did." ▼
Strong Answer

A specific story. The sun moved. The location was half the size. They called an audible with the gaffer, redesigned the setup in twenty minutes, and held the look. Composure and technical depth in the same story.

Weak Answer

A vague recollection without specifics, or no story at all. A working DP who has shot 50 commercial days has five stories like this ready. If they don't, they haven't been on enough sets, or they don't learn from failure.

Q3 "What is your relationship with your gaffer?" ▼
Strong Answer

Names a specific gaffer they work with consistently. Describes the visual shorthand. "I say 'half stop cooler on the key' and she already knows what grip position to use." That shorthand is speed on your shoot day.

Weak Answer

"Whoever production assigns." This DP has not built a team. Every day with a new gaffer is a calibration period you're paying for. Budget an extra 90 minutes to the setup time on every major scene.

Q4 "How do you communicate the look to a colorist you've never worked with?" ▼
Strong Answer

LUTs on set. Written intent notes. Reference frames exported with verbal description. They're thinking about the image past wrap. Your client's finished deliverable is downstream of this answer.

Weak Answer

"I trust the colorist." This DP is exiting the image at wrap. The colorist will make reasonable choices. They will not make the choices the DP had in mind. That gap ends up in your client's inbox.
