03 — The Three Cost Drivers

//
Crew Structure
One person or eight? The crew count is the biggest variable in any budget. A DP doubling as director is $1,200 to $2,500/day. Add a dedicated gaffer ($500 to $900), key grip ($450 to $700), camera operator ($500 to $750), and 1st AC ($450 to $650), and you're at $3,100 to $5,500 per shoot day in labor alone.
[]
Gear Ownership
Whether the production company owns or rents gear changes the economics. An owned camera package worth $30,000 bills at market rental rate ($800 to $2,000/day) and generates margin after 20 to 40 shooting days. Sub-rental carries 15% markup. Either way, the camera line on a bid is never what you think it is.
--
Post Scope
Post is 30 to 40% of a typical budget. Editorial, color grading by a dedicated colorist, sound design, music licensing, and revision rounds all add up. A $40,000 shoot with $6,000 in post is incomplete. The same shoot with $14,000 in post produces a deliverable with a different finish. Get the post scope in writing before the shoot starts.

Texas SB22 (effective September 2025) offers up to a 31% cash rebate on qualified Texas production spending for larger film and series projects. For commercial and branded content, the incentive doesn't apply directly, but it reflects the state's sustained investment in building production infrastructure in DFW. The market is deep, and it keeps getting deeper.

05 — Rules for Reading a Production Bid

01

Compare crew counts, not totals. Two bids can differ by $20,000 and still be pricing the same quality of work if one is quoting a 3-person shoot and the other is quoting 8. Ask for an itemized crew list before you compare numbers.

02

Post scope is where surprises live. Get explicit clarity on: how many revision rounds, who does color (editor or dedicated colorist), what music licensing covers, and whether motion graphics are included or scoped separately. These four items account for most post-budget disputes.

03

The production fee (overhead) should be visible. Standard is 20 to 25% on hard costs. If you don't see it as a line item, it's buried in the other line items. One is not more honest than the other, but knowing where it is helps you understand what you're actually buying.

04

Budget toward what the footage has to do. A video for a trade show this quarter needs to be good. A brand anchor piece running for two years needs to be right. Production is the only cost in your marketing stack that's permanent. Ad spend disappears when the campaign ends. The footage stays. The budget that makes sense for each use case is not the same number.
