GLM / Production Intelligence

What Does a Director of
Photography Actually Do?

The camera records. The DP decides what it sees. Three departments, two production phases, and every invisible decision that separates cinema from footage.

01 / Command Structure

Three Departments.
One Person at the Top.

A DP does not work alone. The role commands camera, lighting, and grip simultaneously. Understanding this structure explains the day rate. You are not paying one person. You are paying the person who directs three crews in real time.

Camera Department

Answers directly to DP

The DP selects the camera system, builds the lens package, and makes every choice about how images are captured. Sensor size, dynamic range, color science, recording format. These are not vendor decisions. They are storytelling decisions.

The camera department captures the image the DP has designed. Operator, AC, loader. All executing the DP's visual plan.

Roles
Camera Operator (B Camera / 2nd Unit)
Executes movement and framing on additional cameras. Takes direction from DP on angle, speed, and composition intent.
1st Assistant Camera (Focus Puller)
Maintains critical focus in real time. Builds the camera, manages lens swaps, tracks focus marks. Tactically critical.
2nd Assistant Camera (Loader)
Manages media, slates, camera reports. Keeps the camera fed and documented.
DIT (Digital Imaging Technician)
Manages the color pipeline on set. Applies LUTs, checks exposure on scope, backs up media. The DP's technical color lieutenant.

Lighting Department

Gaffer interprets DP's design

The DP designs the lighting scheme. The gaffer figures out how to build it electrically and directs the electric crew. On a commercial set, the lighting setup often consumes 60% of the shoot day. The DP is the reason it looks the way it does.

The DP calls the position, quality, and intensity of every source. The gaffer solves the power and rigging to deliver it.

Roles
Gaffer (Chief Lighting Technician)
First lieutenant for electricity. Translates the DP's lighting concept into physical equipment and electrical solutions. Commands the electric crew.
Best Boy Electric
Manages the electric crew's labor, equipment, and logistics. Gaffer's direct report. Runs the day-to-day of the electric department.
Electricians (Set Lighting Technicians)
Place, rig, and power lighting instruments under gaffer and best boy direction. Execute the physical setup the DP has called for.
Generator Operator
On location shoots without facility power. Manages fuel, load balancing, and the electrical supply the entire set depends on.

Grip Department

Key grip: DP's mechanical counterpart

Grip is the subtractive and mechanical side of image-making. Flags that kill spill, bounce cards that redirect light, diffusion frames that soften sources, and all the rigging that holds camera and lighting in place.

Most clients have never heard of the grip department. It is frequently the difference between a controlled image and a compromised one.

Roles
Key Grip
DP's mechanical counterpart. Commands camera movement systems (dollies, cranes, Steadicam), lighting rigging, and the entire subtractive light control toolkit.
Best Boy Grip
Manages grip crew labor and equipment inventory. Key grip's direct report.
Dolly Grip
Operates the dolly system. Executes every push-in, pull-out, and tracking move with precision. One of the most skilled physical jobs on set.
Grips
Set the flags, cutters, silks, and bounce cards that shape and subtract light under key grip direction.
02 / Pre-Production

The Most Valuable Work
Happens Before Day One.

Every hour a DP spends in prep eliminates three hours of problem-solving on set. The tech scout, the shot list, the equipment build. These are not overhead. They are the production's insurance policy.

Pre-Production
Location Tech Scout
▶ What happens on a scout
The DP walks every location with the director and production designer. The questions being answered: Where is the sun at call time and at wrap. What natural sources exist and can be motivated. What electrical infrastructure is available. What problems exist that need to be solved before the shoot day. A DP who skips the scout is guessing on the day. Guessing costs money.
Pre-Production
Shot List & Director Collaboration
▶ More than just angles
The DP and director build the shot list together. Not just "what angles" but why each shot serves the story. What focal length creates the right emotional distance. Whether a push-in or a locked frame carries the moment. Whether a handheld or Steadicam serves the scene's energy. This is where the visual language of the project gets decided before a single frame is recorded.
Pre-Production
Department Coordination Meetings
▶ Color palettes and wardrobe checks
DP, director, producer, production designer, wardrobe. Color palettes get coordinated. Wardrobe colors get checked against background choices and skin tones. If the wardrobe is the same saturation as the background, the DP knows before the shoot day and solves it in pre-production instead of on set. These meetings are why commercial images look composed rather than assembled.
Pre-Production
Camera Prep & Sensor Testing
▶ Building the imaging pipeline
The DP builds and tests the camera package before the shoot. Lens resolution tests, sensor calibration, color science decisions, LUT development, media workflow verification. A camera problem discovered during prep costs an afternoon. The same problem discovered on set costs a production day. Prep is the most cost-effective thing a DP does.
Production
Blocking & Camera Placement
▶ Where the camera lives
The director tells the actors where to move. The DP determines where the camera goes to make that movement meaningful. Camera height, angle, lens choice, depth of field. None of these are technical afterthoughts. A camera placed at eye level communicates equality. Placed low communicates power. Placed high communicates vulnerability. Every placement is an editorial decision.
Production
Lighting Design & Execution
▶ Hundreds of micro-decisions
More fill. Pull the unit back six inches. Add half a stop of diffusion. Flag that spill off the wall. Bring in a negative fill on the shadow side. These are not random adjustments. Each one shifts what the frame communicates. Over the course of a shoot day, a DP makes hundreds of these decisions in real time. Each one is a choice about how the image reads.
Production
Exposure & Color Pipeline
▶ From sensor to final grade
The DP sets the exposure and manages the color science pipeline from sensor to display. On a RAW-capture commercial, the DP communicates the visual intent to the colorist through reference frames, LUTs, or written notes. The grade in post is built on what the DP captured on set. A DP who does not think about the grade is leaving half the image on the table.

05 / The Business Case

The Rate Is Not for
the Camera. It Is for the System.

DFW commercial DP rates span from $900/day to $3,500/day. The variable is not equipment. Any working DP can rent gear. The variable is the three-department system described above, how well it runs, and what the DP's visual design looks like.
